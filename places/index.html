Places Town hubs · local geography · insider terrain

The Peninsula, town by town.

The Peninsula has twenty distinct localities — from working harbour towns on Western Port to winery hamlets in the hinterland to white-sand ocean villages at the tip. No two towns are alike. This hub organises all of them by subregion so you know which to pair and which to avoid putting in the same day.

Arthur's Seat · Storm over Port Phillip Bay

Why towns matter here

Most people come to the Mornington Peninsula for a category — beaches, wine, hot springs, golf — and pick accommodation that looks close to that category. That usually works out fine. But the Peninsula is specific in a way that categories flatten: the beach towns on Port Phillip Bay are calm, family-tilted, and good in summer; the ocean-side towns face Bass Strait and suit people who like dramatic coastline and solitude; the hinterland ridge is where serious food and wine happens; the Western Port fringe is quieter, underrated, and where artists and racehorses live.

Understanding the subregions changes what you book. Staying in Portsea when you want wineries is a 40-minute drive in the wrong direction. Staying in Red Hill when you want beaches puts you 20 minutes from any coast. Read the subregion intros below, use the town cards to shortlist, then go deep in the individual pages.

Hinterland

Red Hill Plateau · village

Red Hill

Red Hill sits at the cool, misty heart of the Mornington Peninsula hinterland, perched on a basalt plateau where morning fog and afternoon light conspire to make this some of the most coveted pinot noir country in Australia. The plateau runs between the bay and the ocean, high enough to catch the cool Southern Ocean influence that makes the wines sing. A village by any strict measure - a general store, a monthly market, some of Victoria's finest restaurants tucked behind vineyard gates - Red Hill nonetheless organises the whole upper Peninsula around itself. Drives here wind through ferny gullies and open onto views that stop you mid-sentence. The Saturday market is worth planning a weekend around. The winery restaurant circuit - Ten Minutes by Tractor, Montalto, Principia, Crittenden - is the strongest such cluster in the state.

Red Hill Plateau · village

Main Ridge

Main Ridge is the highest part of the Peninsula wine country, and the most forgiving to pinot noir. A few degrees cooler than Red Hill proper, wetter in winter, and foggy on the right mornings - if you drew the perfect place to grow cool-climate grapes in Victoria, it would look something like this. There is no real village centre, just a crossroads, a hall, and a handful of the most serious cellar doors on the Peninsula scattered through ferny side roads. Main Ridge rewards people who want to understand wine country rather than just photograph it: the single-vineyard bottles from this sub-region are consistently among the region's best, the estates are small and run by the people who own them, and nobody is trying to sell you a wedding venue. Come for a morning tasting at one of the serious producers, then drive ten minutes in any direction for lunch.

Red Hill Plateau · village

Merricks

Merricks is where the Peninsula's wine country starts to feel private. There is almost no main street to speak of, just a low-slung hall, winding vineyard roads, and a cluster of estates and restaurants that reward people who know where to turn off. The light is softer here than in Red Hill proper and the bay feels unexpectedly close. It is one of the Peninsula's best places to stay if your idea of a weekend away means vines outside the window, lunch that goes too long, and very little noise.

Red Hill Plateau · village

Balnarring

Balnarring is the village that locals still treat as their own - a cluster of shops, a Thursday morning farmers' market, a bookshop that has outlasted all reasonable expectations, and a road network that somehow always seems to lead back to a cellar door. It sits on the eastern edge of the Peninsula's wine country, closer to Western Port than Port Phillip, and that changes the character: the light is softer, the crowds are thinner, and the vineyards here tend to be smaller family operations rather than destination estates. Balnarring is where you go when you want a Peninsula weekend that feels like the way things used to work before the region became a brand. The beach is genuine and quiet, the coffee is unpretentious, and the drive in from Tedesca or Montalto takes fifteen minutes of roads you've mostly never seen.

Hinterland · village

Moorooduc

Moorooduc is the quiet, slightly eastern edge of the Peninsula's wine country - closer to the railway that still occasionally runs steam trains on weekends than to the ridge road, and far enough off the tourist loop that a morning here feels almost private. The vineyards are smaller, the landscape flatter, the soils different enough to grow serious chardonnay that people outside the Peninsula still underrate. A handful of committed producers - Moorooduc Estate, Barmah Park, Onannon in the next village across - have turned this pocket into one of the most interesting single-vineyard collections in the state. Use Moorooduc for a slow morning cellar-door run before heading up to Red Hill for lunch.

Hinterland · village

Tuerong

Tuerong is the wine country's back door - a rolling fold of hills east of the Moorooduc flats where some of the Peninsula's most interesting vineyards have quietly put down roots. Yabby Lake sits at the centre of it, a Halliday Top 100 winery with one of the most relaxed cellar-door programs on the Peninsula, but the same roads will take you to Dexter Wines, Dromana Estate's newer site and a clutch of low-volume producers whose fruit ends up in bottles you'll see in the best wine bars in Melbourne. The landscape is softer here - more cattle than vines if you take the wrong turn - which is part of why a morning spent looking for cellar doors in Tuerong feels like travel rather than tourism.

Bay Coast

Bay Coast — Mornington to Rosebud

Mornington, Mount Martha, Safety Beach, Dromana, and Rosebud face Port Phillip Bay along the peninsula's eastern edge. The water is calm, the swimming is safe, the bathing boxes are photogenic, and the summer crowds are real. Mornington anchors the northern end as the most town-like of the lot — gallery, market, decent coffee, a working marina.

Bayside · town

Mornington

Mornington is the gateway town, and for a long time that's all it was - the first real stop on the bay road down from Melbourne, somewhere to buy petrol or lunch before pressing on to wineries and beaches further south. That undersells it now. Over the past decade Mornington has quietly developed into one of the best day-out towns on the Peninsula: a genuinely good main street, a Wednesday farmers' market on the Esplanade, a waterfront stretch of bathing boxes and foreshore pools, and a dining scene that ranges from casual brunch rooms to serious modern kitchens. It's also the Peninsula's most practical base if you want proximity to everything - north to Mount Martha's beach, south to Red Hill's vineyards, and fifteen minutes in either direction to the coast. Come for the Wednesday market if you can, walk the Esplanade at dusk, and treat the town as a whole day rather than a waypoint.

Bayside · town

Dromana

Dromana is the town at the foot of Arthurs Seat, and the practical hinge between the bay road and the hinterland behind it. Look one way and the long shallow beach runs uninterrupted toward Safety Beach; look the other and the land climbs fast into pinot country. Because of that geography, Dromana has always carried more than its weight - it is where many of the Peninsula's founding wineries planted their first vines in the 1970s, where Crittenden and the Dromana Estate groundstock still sit, and where the ferry from the ocean side of the ridge puts locals back on the bay in fifteen minutes. The town itself is unpretentious: a foreshore, a pier, a post-war main drag quietly modernising. Use it as a base for Red Hill without paying Red Hill prices, swim in the morning, drive up for lunch, and come back down in time for a sunset on the beach.

Bayside · town

Hastings

Hastings sits on the eastern, working side of the Peninsula - the Western Port side, with its mangroves, mudflats, and fishing fleet. It is not a tourist town and never has tried to be, which is exactly what makes it useful. The fishermen's co-op here supplies some of the best seafood in Melbourne, the mussel farms offshore are among the largest sustainable producers in Australia, and if you are driving back from French Island or heading down to Flinders the long way round, Hastings is the place to stop for fish and chips eaten on the pier. The town itself is unvarnished in a way the western Peninsula no longer is, and that counts for something.

Bayside · village

Mount Martha

Mount Martha is the Peninsula's best-kept secret for people who only want to do one thing: stop. This is swimming-beach country - a long, gentle curve of pale sand with bathing boxes at the northern end, south-facing enough to catch afternoon sun, and sheltered enough from the wind to be usable almost nine months of the year. The village proper sits up on the hill, compact and quiet, with a bakery, a few cafes, and enough infrastructure to get through a weekend without ever needing to drive further than the beach. South of the village, the coastal track winds toward the Briars wetlands and eventually down to Mornington; north, it runs into the foreshore reserve and the bathing boxes. It is the simplest, cleanest Peninsula day on Port Phillip: swim, walk, coffee, repeat.

Bayside · town

Rosebud

Rosebud is the largest of the bayside towns and the one locals use when they actually want something done - a supermarket, a hardware store, an everyday main street that has not yet been aestheticised into a postcard. That practicality is also its charm. The beach is long and genuinely good, with a sheltered swimming area and grass right up to the sand; the jetty is a quiet fishing spot at dusk; and a string of ethnic kitchens along Point Nepean Road provide the kind of honest, unheralded lunches that make a weekend on the Peninsula feel less like a brochure and more like a real place. Use Rosebud as your resupply stop, but don't rush through it.

Bayside · town

Safety Beach

Safety Beach is the long shallow crescent between Mount Martha and Dromana - the kind of bay beach where the water is calm enough for very small children and the ridge behind it is steep enough to put you among vineyards in under ten minutes. For most of its history it was a quiet caravan-and-holiday-house stretch; in the last decade it has slowly sharpened, with a marina at the western end, a handful of smarter houses lining the foreshore, and a couple of genuinely good food stops using the view properly. It is a useful place to stay for anyone who wants a base that works equally for beach mornings and hinterland afternoons.

Tip & ocean

Sorrento to the Tip

Blairgowrie, Sorrento, and Portsea occupy the peninsula's narrow western tip, with Point Nepean National Park sealing the end. Limestone cliffs, white-sand ocean beaches facing Bass Strait, a ferry crossing to Queenscliff, and heritage buildings from Victoria's earliest settlement attempt. Sorrento is the prettiest town on the Peninsula. Portsea is the richest. Blairgowrie is the best-kept secret.

Tip · town

Sorrento

Sorrento is where the Peninsula narrows to a point and the social temperature rises. This is old-money beach culture done with increasing sophistication - limestone cliffs, a ferry to Queenscliff across the Heads, ocean baths carved from rock at low tide - alongside a main street that has quietly evolved into something genuinely worth driving for. The summer crowd is real and relentless, but the town earns it. Off-season Sorrento is a revelation: the light changes, the restaurants empty out to their best selves, and you can walk the ocean beach from one end to the other without encountering anyone but locals. The back beach at dusk is one of the best things on the Peninsula, full stop. Stay at least two nights.

Back Beaches · town

Rye

Rye is the pivot point of the lower Peninsula - the last town before Sorrento and Portsea, the first one where the landscape really opens up to the Southern Ocean. The front beach is a long bay-calm stretch with a pier and a century of summer memory behind it; the back beach is where you drive to in ten minutes to remind yourself that the ocean side is an entirely different country. In between sits a main street that has quietly improved itself over the past five years, a brewery precinct in the hills above the town, and Peninsula Hot Springs close enough to slot into an afternoon without rearranging the day. Rye is where families have always come to do nothing in particular, and that remains the right instinct.

Tip · zone

Point Nepean

Point Nepean is the Peninsula at its most exposed and historically strange: quarantine station buildings facing the bay, old gun emplacements buried in coastal scrub, and walking tracks that keep revealing a new view across Port Phillip Heads just when you think the drama has peaked. It is less a single attraction than a whole day of edges - military ruins, tea-tree, pale water, and the sense that Melbourne has fallen clean away behind you. Go early, wear proper shoes, and give yourself time to keep stopping at the lookouts.

Tip · village

Portsea

Portsea is the Peninsula's quiet trophy. Where Sorrento turns on the theatre, Portsea keeps its back to the bay and its gates closed - this is old-money country, where the holiday houses were built before anyone called them beach houses and the golf club is older than most Australian cities. The village itself is barely a village: a pub, a general store, a scattering of shops around the foreshore, and a short walk down to a jetty that still looks essentially as it did a century ago. But push past the surface and Portsea is the entry point for the whole south-western tip of the Peninsula: Point Nepean is next door, the back-beach surf is five minutes away, and the front beach is one of the most protected swims on Port Phillip. Come for a long lunch at the pub, a quiet morning walk before the day-trippers find Sorrento, or a summer weekend that stays deliberately low-key.

Back Beaches · town

Blairgowrie

Blairgowrie is the quieter neighbour between Rye and Sorrento - the stretch of front beach that still feels like a holiday pattern most of the Peninsula gave up on. The yacht club runs a working moorings marina that defines the skyline; the foreshore is unbroken sand you can swim off without fighting a crowd; and the shops are mostly limited to a pub, a bakery, a couple of cafés and a deli, which is most of what you need. The back beach (Bridgewater Bay) is five minutes inland and quieter still. Blairgowrie rewards the kind of weekend that's built around walking the foreshore, reading on a beach towel, and not making a plan until dinner. It's the last town before the tourist-district tone of Sorrento and the first one where families still outnumber influencers.

Ocean Coast · cape

Cape Schanck

Cape Schanck is where the Peninsula finally runs out of land, dropping off the plateau into Bass Strait with a drama that still takes first-timers by surprise. The 1859 lighthouse stands on the last headland, the boardwalk threads down through tea-tree to a basalt beach that disappears at high tide, and on a clear afternoon the horizon carries nothing but open ocean all the way to Tasmania. This is the Peninsula's most cinematic short walk, and the cleanest case for why the southern end is worth the drive beyond the vineyards. Come for the lighthouse and the lookout, stay for the Bushrangers Bay walk west along the coast, and time your arrival for the last two hours of daylight.

Ocean Coast · town

Flinders

Flinders sits on the wild southern edge of the Peninsula and carries itself with a kind of salt-air confidence. The village is compact but smart - a bakery, a handful of good shops, an old pub, and a coastline that feels a world away from the bay-facing polish further north. Base yourself here if you want your mornings to begin with wind off Bass Strait, your afternoons with a drive back inland for lunch, and your evenings under a harder, cleaner sky.

Ocean Coast · village

Shoreham

Shoreham barely qualifies as a village, and that is exactly its charm. A store, a hall, a tangle of narrow roads threading down to one of the loveliest small beaches on the Peninsula, and a handful of vineyards and olive groves that quietly produce some of the region's best fruit. The beach is family-scaled and protected enough for children, the bush behind it runs into Point Leo, and the drive up onto the ridge puts you in cellar door country within ten minutes. This is where locals go when the southern beaches start to feel loud and the long weekend crowd has swallowed Sorrento. Park once, walk to the beach, and stay until the light goes.

In between sits a main street that has quietly improved itself over the past five years, a brewery precinct in the hills above the town, and Peninsula Hot Springs close enough to slot into an afternoon without rearranging the day. Rye is where families have always come to do nothing in particular, and that remains the right instinct.

Bayside · town

Safety Beach

Safety Beach is the long shallow crescent between Mount Martha and Dromana - the kind of bay beach where the water is calm enough for very small children and the ridge behind it is steep enough to put you among vineyards in under ten minutes. For most of its history it was a quiet caravan-and-holiday-house stretch; in the last decade it has slowly sharpened, with a marina at the western end, a handful of smarter houses lining the foreshore, and a couple of genuinely good food stops using the view properly. It is a useful place to stay for anyone who wants a base that works equally for beach mornings and hinterland afternoons.

Ocean Coast · village

Shoreham

Shoreham barely qualifies as a village, and that is exactly its charm. A store, a hall, a tangle of narrow roads threading down to one of the loveliest small beaches on the Peninsula, and a handful of vineyards and olive groves that quietly produce some of the region's best fruit. The beach is family-scaled and protected enough for children, the bush behind it runs into Point Leo, and the drive up onto the ridge puts you in cellar door country within ten minutes. This is where locals go when the southern beaches start to feel loud and the long weekend crowd has swallowed Sorrento. Park once, walk to the beach, and stay until the light goes.

Tip · town

Sorrento

Sorrento is where the Peninsula narrows to a point and the social temperature rises. This is old-money beach culture done with increasing sophistication - limestone cliffs, a ferry to Queenscliff across the Heads, ocean baths carved from rock at low tide - alongside a main street that has quietly evolved into something genuinely worth driving for. The summer crowd is real and relentless, but the town earns it. Off-season Sorrento is a revelation: the light changes, the restaurants empty out to their best selves, and you can walk the ocean beach from one end to the other without encountering anyone but locals. The back beach at dusk is one of the best things on the Peninsula, full stop. Stay at least two nights.

Hinterland · village

Tuerong

Tuerong is the wine country's back door - a rolling fold of hills east of the Moorooduc flats where some of the Peninsula's most interesting vineyards have quietly put down roots. Yabby Lake sits at the centre of it, a Halliday Top 100 winery with one of the most relaxed cellar-door programs on the Peninsula, but the same roads will take you to Dexter Wines, Dromana Estate's newer site and a clutch of low-volume producers whose fruit ends up in bottles you'll see in the best wine bars in Melbourne. The landscape is softer here - more cattle than vines if you take the wrong turn - which is part of why a morning spent looking for cellar doors in Tuerong feels like travel rather than tourism.

FAQ

Which town should I stay in on the Mornington Peninsula?

It depends on what you're doing. For wineries and restaurants: Red Hill or Merricks. For the most dramatic scenery and beach options: Sorrento or Portsea. For a family-friendly bay beach base: Rosebud, Dromana, or Mount Martha. For something understated and local: Flinders or Balnarring. Read where to base yourself for the full breakdown.

How many towns can I cover in a day trip from Melbourne?

Realistically two, maybe three if they're adjacent. Red Hill and Mornington pair well. Sorrento and Portsea are 10 minutes apart. Cape Schanck and Flinders work together. Don't try to cross from the bay coast to the tip in a single day trip — it's 90 minutes of driving.

Are all the towns accessible by public transport?

Most towns have bus connections from Frankston (which has a train from Melbourne). Sorrento, Portsea, Dromana, Rosebud, and Rye are covered by bus route 788. Flinders, Balnarring, and Shoreham are on route 782. Hastings has its own train station on the Stony Point line. Moorooduc, Tuerong, Red Hill, Main Ridge, and Merricks have limited or no public transport — a car is required.

What's the difference between the bay beaches and the ocean beaches?

Bay beaches (Port Phillip side) are calm, shallow, and safe for young children — think bathing boxes and paddling. Ocean beaches (Bass Strait side) have surf, rips, and a wild energy — better for surfing and dramatic walks. Sorrento and Portsea uniquely have both: a front beach on the bay, a back beach on the ocean.

Is the Mornington Peninsula worth visiting in winter?

Strongly yes. The restaurants are quieter, the cellar doors are unhurried, and the dramatic ocean beaches are at their best in cold weather. Winter is when Peninsula Insider recommends Flinders and Cape Schanck most aggressively. Read winter on the Peninsula for the itinerary.
