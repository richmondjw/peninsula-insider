Hinterland · village

Tuerong

Tuerong is the wine country's back door - a rolling fold of hills east of the Moorooduc flats where some of the Peninsula's most interesting vineyards have quietly put down roots. Yabby Lake sits at the centre of it, a Halliday Top 100 winery with one of the most relaxed cellar-door programs on the Peninsula, but the same roads will take you to Dexter Wines, Dromana Estate's newer site and a clutch of low-volume producers whose fruit ends up in bottles you'll see in the best wine bars in Melbourne. The landscape is softer here - more cattle than vines if you take the wrong turn - which is part of why a morning spent looking for cellar doors in Tuerong feels like travel rather than tourism.

Nearby places worth knowing next

Because a Peninsula trip is always made up of two or three pockets, not one.

Hinterland · village

Moorooduc

Moorooduc is the quiet, slightly eastern edge of the Peninsula's wine country - closer to the railway that still occasionally runs steam trains on weekends than to the ridge road, and far enough off the tourist loop that a morning here feels almost private. The vineyards are smaller, the landscape flatter, the soils different enough to grow serious chardonnay that people outside the Peninsula still underrate. A handful of committed producers - Moorooduc Estate, Barmah Park, Onannon in the next village across - have turned this pocket into one of the most interesting single-vineyard collections in the state. Use Moorooduc for a slow morning cellar-door run before heading up to Red Hill for lunch.

Bayside · town

Mornington

Mornington is the gateway town, and for a long time that's all it was - the first real stop on the bay road down from Melbourne, somewhere to buy petrol or lunch before pressing on to wineries and beaches further south. That undersells it now. Over the past decade Mornington has quietly developed into one of the best day-out towns on the Peninsula: a genuinely good main street, a Wednesday farmers' market on the Esplanade, a waterfront stretch of bathing boxes and foreshore pools, and a dining scene that ranges from casual brunch rooms to serious modern kitchens. It's also the Peninsula's most practical base if you want proximity to everything - north to Mount Martha's beach, south to Red Hill's vineyards, and fifteen minutes in either direction to the coast. Come for the Wednesday market if you can, walk the Esplanade at dusk, and treat the town as a whole day rather than a waypoint.

Red Hill Plateau · village

Red Hill

Red Hill sits at the cool, misty heart of the Mornington Peninsula hinterland, perched on a basalt plateau where morning fog and afternoon light conspire to make this some of the most coveted pinot noir country in Australia. The plateau runs between the bay and the ocean, high enough to catch the cool Southern Ocean influence that makes the wines sing. A village by any strict measure - a general store, a monthly market, some of Victoria's finest restaurants tucked behind vineyard gates - Red Hill nonetheless organises the whole upper Peninsula around itself. Drives here wind through ferny gullies and open onto views that stop you mid-sentence. The Saturday market is worth planning a weekend around. The winery restaurant circuit - Ten Minutes by Tractor, Montalto, Principia, Crittenden - is the strongest such cluster in the state.
