The Peninsula Beach Swimming Guide: Which Beach for Which Swim, From Bay to Back Beach

Thirty kilometres of Port Phillip coastline, fifteen kilometres of Bass Strait back beaches, and four distinct swimming moods that most visitors cannot tell apart. A proper guide to where to actually swim on the Mornington Peninsula, by conditions and by purpose.

By The Peninsula Insider
BeachSwimSummerFamilyBayBack BeachesExplore
A swimmer pushing off from the shallow pale sand of a Peninsula bay beach on a calm morning

The Peninsula has more publicly accessible coastline than almost any region within ninety minutes of Melbourne, and one of the quiet scandals of how it is usually written about is that the beaches get treated as interchangeable.

They are not. A Peninsula swim at Safety Beach is an entirely different experience from a swim at Sorrento Back Beach, which is a different experience again from a sandbar swim at Rye or a bathing-box swim at Mount Martha. Getting the right beach for the right kind of swim is one of those under-appreciated Peninsula skills that separates a good day out from one where everyone ends up damp, cold, and quietly miffed that the water was not what they imagined.

This is the guide. It is organised by what you actually want out of the swim, not by geography.

The four Peninsula swim moods

Every swim on the Peninsula falls into roughly one of four categories. Pick the mood first, and the beach selects itself.

1. The calm-water family swim. Shallow, warm, safe, easy parking, often with a kiosk, almost always on the bay side. This is the default family swim. The Peninsula has more of these than any other kind.

2. The serious open-water swim. Deeper, cooler, no sandbar chaos — the swim you actually want to do laps in. Also bay side, but at the beaches with a steeper bank.

3. The dramatic-scenery ocean walk-and-wade. Back beaches. Bass Strait. Not a swim in any meaningful sense, but a coastal experience with a cold plunge in it if you are brave. The surf makes a proper swim dangerous for anyone not local and experienced.

4. The infrastructure swim. Ocean pools, jetty ladders, sea baths — engineered swims that let you get into the deeper water safely. The Peninsula has two great examples and a couple of minor ones.

The mistake most visitors make is defaulting to mood three — “I want to see the back beaches” — and then trying to swim there. The back beaches are for walking and looking, not swimming unless you know the surf.

Bay side: where to actually swim

The bay side of the Peninsula runs roughly from Mornington in the north to Portsea at the tip, and the entire thirty-kilometre stretch is the Peninsula’s real swimming coast. The water is warmer, calmer, shallower and safer than the ocean side, and the beaches are strung between small towns so that the parking, the coffee and the shade are almost always within a five-minute walk.

Here are the beaches that matter, north to south, and what each is best for.

Mount Martha Beach — the default family swim

Mount Martha Beach is the benchmark Peninsula family swim, and the reason it gets recommended so often is that it earns it. A long, shallow arc of pale sand, bathing boxes at the northern end, a single kiosk, easy parking along Esplanade, and enough length that the crowds thin out if you walk five minutes from the car park. In autumn, the water is still wading-warm into April. In mid-summer it is the most reliably pleasant swim on the Peninsula for children aged three to nine.

Best for: Families with small children, first-time visitors, and anyone who wants the Peninsula-beach postcard without engineering it.

Avoid if: You want deep water for laps, or you are arriving after 10:30am on a 35-degree day without a parking plan.

Dromana and Safety Beach — the long walks, the long sandbars

Dromana Beach and the adjacent Safety Beach Foreshore are the Peninsula’s longest bay-side sand stretches, and their defining feature is the sandbar that extends out from the waterline to more than a hundred metres on a low tide. On a calm summer morning you can walk half-way to the horizon on shin-deep water with the bay going perfectly still around you, which is its own kind of swim. This is not deep-water territory, but it is the best place on the Peninsula for paddle boarding, casual kite-surfing, or just drifting on the water with a child on your back.

The kiosk at Dromana is small. The car park is big. Both fill early in peak summer but are largely empty outside it.

Best for: Sandbar walks, stand-up paddleboarding, families with toddlers, older swimmers who are not after depth.

Avoid if: You want a clear horizon for lap swimming or the wind is up from the north — the chop flattens the whole stretch.

Portsea and Sorrento Front Beach — the deeper bay swim

Further south, the bay coast gets deeper quickly, and this is where the serious open-water swim lives. Portsea Front Beach in particular is the swim for anyone who is doing laps or wants to step off the sand and be in waist-deep water within ten metres. The sand shelves more steeply, the water is cleaner because of the proximity to the Heads, and in the mornings before the ferries start it is one of the most beautiful quiet swims in Victoria.

Sorrento Front Beach is the same basic story: quick drop-off, clean clear water, a short stroll from the village. The Sorrento Baths (the cute bay-side pavilion with the enclosed swim) are a good option for less confident swimmers who still want the deeper bay experience without open water.

Best for: Adult swimmers, morning laps, cleaner deeper bay water.

Avoid if: You are bringing a toddler who wants to paddle — Mount Martha is a better fit for that purpose.

Balnarring Beach — the other family default

Over on the Western Port side, Balnarring Beach is the Peninsula’s other great family swim. It is quieter than anywhere on the Port Phillip side, backed by ti-tree scrub rather than built infrastructure, no kiosk, and a considerably more local feel. The water is slightly darker because the Western Port sands are finer, but the swim itself is equivalent in temperature and gentleness to Mount Martha. Bring your own chairs and a thermos.

Best for: Families who want the quietest possible bay swim, local-feel picnics, combining with the Balnarring Market or a Balnarring Pub lunch.

Avoid if: You need kiosk food or public toilets close to your towel — facilities at Balnarring are minimal.

Back beaches: walk, do not swim

The Peninsula’s southern ocean coast — Sorrento Back Beach, Rye Ocean Beach, Gunnamatta, St Andrews — is one of the great short-coast drives in southern Australia. Black reefs, high cliffs, cold water, proper surf. It is also the part of the Peninsula where people without local knowledge most often get into trouble in the water. The rips are real, the sets come in long, and the currents along the reef shelves at the ends of most of these beaches are not suitable for swimming-pool-fit holidaymakers.

The rule is simple: visit the back beaches in the morning or late afternoon for the walk, the light, the cliffs and the rock pools. Do not plan a swim there unless you are an experienced surf swimmer and there are lifeguards on duty (which, outside summer weekends and school holidays, is usually not the case).

Sorrento Back Beach is the most walkable of the back beaches and the most photographed. The short sand is enclosed at both ends by dramatic basalt reefs. The walk along the top of the cliffs toward the Pillars or down to Diamond Bay is one of the best thirty minutes on the Peninsula. The Sorrento Ocean Baths — technically part of the back beach precinct — are a small natural-rock pool at the base of the cliffs that can be safely waded in on a calm low tide, and are worth the walk regardless.

Rye Ocean Beach and Gunnamatta Ocean Beach are longer, flatter sand stretches with considerably bigger and more serious surf. Both are patrolled in peak season. Both are surf-swim-level swims, not family-swim options.

If you have a genuine swim itch on the back-beach side, the best and safest option is the small protected cove just west of Bushrangers Bay, reached via the walking track from Cape Schanck — but this is a walker’s swim, not a drop-the-towel family afternoon.

The infrastructure options

Two places on the Peninsula are built specifically to let you get into deeper water safely.

The Baths Sorrento — at the pier end of the Sorrento foreshore — combines a restaurant, a small sheltered swimming area, and a waterfront terrace that is one of the best ways to combine a swim with a lunch. The swim itself is not epic, but the package is distinctive.

The Sorrento Ocean Baths on the back-beach side are the rock-cut pools at the base of the cliffs, only swimmable on calm low tides but one of the genuinely unique experiences on the whole Peninsula. Check a tide chart before you go and never attempt them in a big swell.

The Mornington Pier area and Mothers Beach jetty also have low-ladder access to deeper bay water, and local masters-swim groups use them year-round.

A quick decision tree

  • Swim with small children, first-time visitor: Mount Martha. No other answer.
  • Swim on a quiet day, avoid the crowds: Balnarring.
  • Adult lap swim, deep clean water: Portsea Front Beach, morning.
  • Paddle board or sandbar walk: Dromana or Safety Beach.
  • Dramatic coastal walk with an optional cold plunge: Sorrento Back Beach or the Sorrento Ocean Baths on a low tide.
  • Swim plus food: The Baths Sorrento, or a Mount Martha morning before coffee in the village.
  • Winter “swim”: You do not want to swim. You want the bathing-box walk at Mount Martha, the Cape Schanck Boardwalk and a hot-springs session to warm back up.

What the guide is for

The point of this piece is not to rank beaches. It is to undo the assumption that they are all variations on the same thing. On the Peninsula they are not. The bay is not the ocean. The shallow family swim at Mount Martha is not the clean deeper swim at Portsea, and neither is the dramatic cliff-and-surf experience at Sorrento Back Beach. Choose the swim you actually want, pick the right coast, and the whole experience gets better immediately.

Which is, in the end, the general rule of the Peninsula: stop trying to do everything, pick the right version, and let the water do the rest.

Beach
Balnarring 60 min · Easy

Balnarring Beach

Balnarring Beach is Western Port's quiet argument against the bay-side beaches most visitors default to. The sand is paler, the water flatter, and the crowd is almost entirely local — a few families on summer weekends, a scattering of walkers the rest of the year. The beach itself runs for kilometres, with ti-tree scrub backing the dunes and almost no built infrastructure. This is the Peninsula beach people come to when they want to swim without any of the theatre. Good for a slow afternoon between a Merricks lunch and a Balnarring produce stop. Very good for dusk walks in autumn when the light turns mauve across the water. Go through the Balnarring Beach hamlet for the easiest access point. Bring your own chair. Don't expect a kiosk.

Beach
Dromana 120 min

Dromana Beach

Dromana Beach is a long, gentle arc of sand near the midpoint of the Peninsula — a forgiving bay beach with shallow, calm water and enough stretch to absorb a weekend crowd without losing its charm. The foreshore is backed by lawn and shade trees; the pier runs out into the bay and is a quiet spot for fishing at dawn or dusk. This is the bayside beach to use when you want a straightforward family day without the parking battles of Sorrento or Rye. The town strip behind the beach has enough for an impromptu lunch or a coffee run, and the position puts you in the right place to drive up to the hinterland for an afternoon cellar-door session. Good for swimmers at any standard, especially good for children, and reliably uncrowded outside the peak school-holiday weeks.

Beach
Cape Schanck 60 min

Gunnamatta Ocean Beach

Gunnamatta is long, wild, and remote-feeling despite being accessible from the main road. Powerful surf, huge skies, and almost nobody on the sand outside peak summer. This is not a swimming beach in any sensible sense — the rips are serious, the waves are powerful, and the water temperature is cold most of the year — but it is an extraordinary beach for walking and watching the power of Bass Strait. Surfers love the consistent beach break and you will always see a few sitting out the back if the swell is up. Everyone else comes for the experience of standing on the sand with nothing but ocean ahead of them and feeling properly small. A good late-afternoon stop for the light. Stay on the sand; do not enter the water unless you know exactly what you are doing.
