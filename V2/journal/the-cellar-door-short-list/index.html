The Cellar Door Short List: Five Producers Worth the Appointment

Not the biggest estates, not the flashiest rooms. The five Peninsula cellar doors where the wines — and the people pouring them — actually change how you think about the region.

By The Peninsula Insider
WineRed HillMain RidgeCellar DoorPinot Noir
Five wine glasses lined up for a tasting flight in a Peninsula cellar door

Every time someone asks us for a Peninsula cellar door list we have the same quiet argument with ourselves: the big, beautiful estates with lawns and restaurants are not the best places to taste wine. They are excellent places to spend a long lunch. But the experience of understanding what the Peninsula actually grows — the structural pinot noir, the tight chardonnay, the lesser-known Italian varieties — tends to happen in smaller rooms, at smaller tables, with the people who actually made the wine doing the pouring.

This is that list. Five cellar doors. None of them have a hat. All of them have a waiting list.

1. Kooyong — the benchmark

Kooyong is the single-vineyard producer most serious wine people will name if you ask them to identify the Peninsula’s ceiling. The estate sits on the Main Ridge plateau with roughly forty hectares under vine, split into named blocks that have given the region some of its most collectible bottles. The tasting walks you through side-by-side pinots from adjacent slopes — Haven, Meres, Ferrous, Faultline — and shows you, in under an hour, what terroir actually means in practice.

The cellar door is calm and contemplative. Book the single-vineyard flight. Go midweek. This is where the Peninsula’s argument for seriousness starts.

2. Dexter Wines — the appointment-only secret

Dexter has no signage and no restaurant. The tastings are strictly by appointment. What you get in exchange is the most private, most focused hour in Main Ridge: Tod Dexter or one of his team pouring through a small, deliberately narrow range of pinot noir and chardonnay that has been refined over three decades. The wines are quietly structured and reward cellaring in a way that the bigger trophy estates often don’t.

This is the cellar door for people who want to understand what a Peninsula pinot looks like with four or five years of age on it. Book at least a week ahead.

3. Ocean Eight — the family project

Mike Aylward grew up in Peninsula wine country. His family founded Kooyong. When he decided to start his own label he built Ocean Eight deliberately smaller and more single-minded than any of the big names, and the cellar door reflects that. It is minimal, functional, and entirely focused on the wines.

The Verve and Aylward Pinot Noirs are among the most ageable wines being made on the Peninsula right now. The chardonnays hold their own against the region’s benchmarks. Mike or a family member usually runs the tasting themselves, and the conversation is the second half of the experience.

4. Polperro — the room you want to linger in

Polperro is the one cellar door on this list that is also a pleasant place to spend a whole afternoon. The tasting room is tucked into the vines with views across the ridge, the pours are generous, and the estate has its own restaurant and villa rental next door if you want to stretch the visit into a lunch or a night.

The wines themselves are excellent — a Halliday 97-point estate with a genuine reputation for pinot — and the second label, Pennon Hill, is often the sweet spot for buying. Come for a late morning tasting, walk the vineyard, and decide afterwards whether you want the lunch.

5. Quealy Winemakers — the one with the most soul

Quealy is different. Kathleen Quealy and Kevin McCarthy were early believers in alternative Italian varieties on the Peninsula — pinot grigio, friulano, moscato giallo, and other food-first grapes that made sense in this climate before most locals knew what to do with them — and the cellar door is still grounded in that experimental, quietly pioneering spirit. The room itself is unpretentious to the point of stubbornness. The wines are distinctive and food-friendly. The pricing is fair.

This is where locals send friends who say they want to understand the Peninsula rather than just photograph it. Go before lunch nearby and buy a mixed six on the way out.

The order

If you only have one day for a tasting circuit, do it in this sequence:

  • 10:00 — Quealy Winemakers for the welcome and the Italian varieties
  • 11:30 — Kooyong for the single-vineyard flight
  • 1:30 — Lunch, which is not on this list. See the Red Hill Saturday piece.
  • 3:30 — Ocean Eight for the structured pinots and the family story

Dexter Wines and Polperro are better saved for a second visit, because both reward the kind of unhurried attention you cannot give after three other cellar doors.

The cellar door rule

One rule, non-negotiable: do not taste at more than three cellar doors in a single day. After the third your palate is unreliable, the pours blur together, and you are no longer tasting — you are processing. The Peninsula rewards restraint more than any wine region in Victoria. Three tastings, one lunch, one walk, one good night’s sleep, and you come away with something you actually remember.

Eight cellar doors in a day is a bachelorette party. Three cellar doors in a day is wine country.
