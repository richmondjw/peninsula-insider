Walks on the Mornington Peninsula

The Peninsula Insider approach to walks

Editorial voice, not a trail database.

Parks Victoria has the official facts. AllTrails has user reviews. This section has something neither of them offers: honest characterisation of what each walk is actually like, who it suits, when to go, and how to make it part of a better Peninsula day. We are not neutral about the walks. The Bushrangers Bay walk is exceptional. The Long Point Circuit in Greens Bush is one of the Peninsula's most undervisited good walks. The Cape Schanck boardwalk is short but not to be dismissed. We say so.

Dogs are banned from Mornington Peninsula National Park — including most of the clifftop and trail network. We note this clearly rather than burying it. Accurate is more useful than aspirational.

Coastal & full-day walks

The walks worth clearing an afternoon for

Cape Schanck, Bushrangers Bay, the Two Bays, the Coastal Walk corridor. These are the Peninsula walks that define the region — the ones people come back to.

All walks guide →
Walk
Cape Schanck 600 min · Moderate

Cape Schanck to London Bridge Coastal Walk

The spectacular clifftop traverse from Cape Schanck to London Bridge is the Peninsula's most scenic long coastal walk, and one of the most impressive coastal walks anywhere in Victoria. Twenty-six kilometres of Bass Strait cliff, hidden coves, rugged rock formations, tidal pools, and dense coastal scrub with the Southern Ocean crashing below you the entire way. Orange markers indicate the through-track direction and sections can be walked as shorter day trips rather than the full traverse - Cape Schanck to Bushrangers Bay and back is an excellent half-day; Diamond Bay to London Bridge another. Check tides before setting off: some lower sections flood at high tide and require the inland bypass. Wear proper footwear. Bring water. Do not walk in storm weather - the cliffs here are serious and the weather can turn fast.

Walk
Cape Schanck 600 min · Moderate

Two Bays Walking Track

Two Bays is the Peninsula's signature long-distance walking track - a 26-kilometre one-way trail linking Port Phillip Bay at Dromana with Bass Strait at Cape Schanck, threading through Arthurs Seat State Park, Greens Bush, and Mornington Peninsula National Park along the way. It passes through grasstree stands that are more than two hundred years old, pockets of tea-tree alive with birds, fern gullies that feel more like a wet-forest than a coastal reserve, and open eucalypt forest full of kangaroos at dusk. Most people walk it in sections rather than one push. The best day-walk segments are the Greens Bush middle stretch for the ancient vegetation, and the Cape Schanck end for the ocean cliffs and the lighthouse finish. Fit walkers complete the full traverse in a single day (8-10 hours); most groups take two, with a car shuffle or a friend willing to do a drop-and-collect. One of the genuine walking achievements the Peninsula has to offer. Do it at least once if you live within day-tripping distance.

Walk
Main Ridge 240 min · Moderate

Greens Bush - Two Bays Section

The Greens Bush section of the Two Bays Walking Track is the most rewarding bushwalking segment the Peninsula has - 8.9 kilometres one-way through the largest remnant bushland on the Peninsula, with ancient grasstrees (some more than two hundred years old), pockets of dense tea-tree alive with birds, fern gullies, and open eucalypt forest interrupted only by the sound of the occasional kangaroo. This is where the walking really slows down. The canopy closes overhead in places, the understory birds work the tea-tree continuously, and the track passes through a landscape that looks more or less as it would have before European settlement. Genuinely beautiful, and far quieter than the coastal sections of the same trail. Walk it one-way with a car shuffle, or do a shorter circuit from the Greens Bush Carpark off Baldrys Road. Best in autumn and spring.

Walk
Cape Schanck 180 min · Moderate

The Two Bays Walking Track

The Two Bays Walking Track is the Peninsula's answer to a real day hike - a 26-kilometre ridge-and-coast traverse that runs from Cape Schanck on the southern ocean all the way to Dromana on Port Phillip Bay. Most people take it on in sections rather than end-to-end, and that is the smarter version anyway: the southern leg from Cape Schanck to Bushrangers Bay and back is the most dramatic, and the middle section through Greens Bush is the most forested and quiet. Go in autumn when the weather is clear and the light is already slanting. Take water, take a map, and take longer than the park service estimate suggests. Finish at one of the Main Ridge cellar doors if you are walking north, or at the Portsea Hotel if you are walking west. Allow half a day minimum. This is the Peninsula's best argument for treating a walk as the main event of the day, not the filler.

Walk
Sorrento 150 min · Moderate

Coppins Track

Coppins Track runs four kilometres from Sorrento Ocean Beach through clifftop coastal heathland to Diamond Bay, following a route threaded with a century of local history. Interpretive signage along the way reads the landscape for you: the Peninsula's quarantine era, the military history of the area, the natural history of the heath. Diamond Bay is a small, almost-always-empty beach at the far end and an excellent turnaround point for a round trip. The track is more than a walk; it is one of the best ways to understand how this stretch of the Peninsula actually came to look the way it does. Coastal erosion, endemic flora, and the old paths between bays are all visible as you move through. Allow around 2.5 hours return. Best in the cooler months - the exposed heath gets hot in summer midday.

Walk
Mornington 120 min · Easy

Mornington Peninsula Foreshore Walk

The foreshore walk between Mornington and Mount Martha is the Peninsula's most dog-friendly significant walk and one of its most reliably beautiful - seven kilometres one way along the coast, with sweeping Port Phillip Bay views, heritage bathing boxes, clifftop lookouts, and several quiet little beaches you can detour into along the way. Start at Mornington Pier and walk south toward Mount Martha, or reverse the direction for a later-afternoon light run. The track is well-formed throughout, accessible to most fitness levels, and supports everything from a fast morning run to a leisurely family stroll. It is the best long morning walk on the bayside, followed by coffee at either end. For the full seven kilometres, allow 90 minutes walking at a brisk pace or two hours with stops. For a shorter segment, the stretch between Mills Beach and Fossil Beach is particularly good.

Walk
Cape Schanck 90 min · Moderate

Bushrangers Bay Walk

Bushrangers Bay is the walk we send people to when they say they want to see the wilder side of the Peninsula without committing to a full-day hike. The track drops from the Cape Schanck lighthouse precinct through coastal scrub and opens onto a broad crescent of basalt and sand that feels much further from Melbourne than it is. The return climb is enough to justify lunch afterwards, but not so punishing that it tips into chore. Do it in the late afternoon when the light starts to flatten across the water and the whole coastline turns silver.

Walk
Cape Schanck 90 min · Easy

Cape Schanck Lighthouse Walk

The Cape Schanck Lighthouse walk is one of the most dramatic short coastal walks in Victoria. A well-formed boardwalk trail leads from the lighthouse - a working heritage structure dating from 1859 - down to the headland and Pulpit Rock, with extraordinary views over Bass Strait every step of the way. The boardwalk out to the headland is genuinely vertiginous; you are walking on a platform above sheer rock with ocean on both sides. Allow about 90 minutes for the 4-kilometre return. The track is mostly boardwalk and wide gravel and is suitable for most fitness levels. The lighthouse itself is open for tours on selected days and is worth the short additional stop - the view from the top of the tower is among the best on the entire Peninsula. Go early morning for the light or late afternoon for a sunset on the headland. Check wind conditions before the boardwalk; the cape is exposed.

Under an hour

Between lunch and dinner. Or before coffee. Or both.

Walks that fit in the gaps of a Peninsula day without needing to be planned around. All genuinely worth doing — not the short version because the real walk was too far.

Walk
Red Hill 60 min · Easy

Summit Circuit Walk - Arthurs Seat

The Summit Circuit is the ideal short walk for most Peninsula visitors who want the Arthurs Seat view without committing to an all-day trek. A 1.8-kilometre loop at the top of the mountain that guides you past the major points of interest - Seawinds Gardens, the Matthew Flinders Cairn, the William Ricketts sculptures, bay-to-ocean lookouts at multiple points, and the Seawinds Nursery Indigenous Garden - all within an easy hour of walking. The view from the summit is the entire Peninsula in one sweep: Port Phillip Bay on one side, the ridge and the ocean country on the other. On a clear day you can see as far as the You Yangs to the west and the city of Melbourne to the north. Drive or take the Arthurs Seat Eagle (chairlift) to the top. Walk the full circuit. Take a coffee from the summit kiosk on the way back.

Walk
Point Nepean 45 min · Easy

Farnsworth Track

The Farnsworth Track is the Peninsula's most efficient clifftop-view-to-effort ratio: 1.5 kilometres one way between Portsea Ocean Beach and London Bridge, with dramatic Bass Strait cliff views at almost every point along the walk. Walk one way and return along the beach (tide permitting) for a satisfying short circuit, or push on west to Coppins Track for a longer day. This is the walk you do when you have an hour to fill and want the ocean properly in front of you. The track is well-formed, the gradient is forgiving, and the payoff comes quickly. London Bridge itself - a weathered limestone arch in the rock - is the natural turnaround point and worth the short detour off the main track. Good in almost any weather. The clifftop is exposed, so dress for the wind.

Walk + something

Peninsula walks linked to what comes before and after

The best Peninsula walk days are not just about the walk. These are the combinations that make a walk into a proper day out.

Walk + Hot Springs

Bushrangers Bay → Peninsula Hot Springs

The Cape Schanck corridor's best full-day combination. Walk Bushrangers Bay in the morning — 2 hours, back by 11am. Drive 15 minutes to Peninsula Hot Springs for a midday thermal session. Lunch at the springs café or drive to Fingal for options. The walk and the hot springs are both best on weekdays; avoid summer Saturday peak for both.

Walk + Wine

Arthurs Seat → Red Hill Cellar Doors

The Summit Circuit at Arthurs Seat in the morning — 1.8km, 1 hour, with views to Melbourne on a clear day. Then drop into the Red Hill cellar door circuit for the afternoon. Montalto, Pt. Leo Estate, and a dozen others within 15 minutes of the summit. The day structure works perfectly: active morning, wine afternoon.

Walk + Lunch

Coppins Track → Sorrento lunch

Start at Sorrento Ocean Beach, walk the Coppins Track to Diamond Bay (4km, 90 minutes), return, and walk into Sorrento town for lunch. Multiple good options on Ocean Beach Road and the main strip. The walk earns the meal, and the timing works: finish the track by 12:30pm, lunch at 1pm, out by 3pm.

Walk + Stay

Two Bays Walking Trail → overnight

The Two Bays is best broken into two days with overnight camping at Lightwood Creek, or as a point-to-point with a stay at either Dromana or Cape Schanck end. Walking 26km in a single day is possible but compresses the experience into a time trial. The overnight version lets you get the morning light in Greens Bush — the part of the walk most day-walkers miss.

Walk + Sunset

Farnsworth Track → Portsea for sunset

The Farnsworth Track from London Bridge carpark is 1.5km one way — do it in the late afternoon, 4pm in autumn or winter. Arrive at the Portsea Ocean Beach lookout by 5pm. The light on Bass Strait at that hour is extraordinary. Walk back, drive 10 minutes to Portsea Hotel for a drink on the terrace while the sky settles.

Walk + Dog

Dog-friendly foreshore walks

Dogs are banned from the National Park and most major Peninsula walking tracks. The best dog-friendly walks are on the bay-side foreshore: Mornington foreshore, Mount Martha clifftop sections, and the Safety Beach to Dromana route. All on-lead, all accessible, and all within 15 minutes of good cafés. Check current local-council rules before visiting, as off-lead zones change seasonally.

Specific trails

The Peninsula's major walks, properly characterised

Beyond the walk stats — what each track is actually like, when to go, and who it suits.

The Mornington Peninsula Walk (97.5km)

The Peninsula's full circuit walk — Eight to ten days, combining the Two Bays Trail, the Coastal Walk, the Point Nepean Walk, and the Bay Trail. Often talked about as a challenge to "complete." The more enjoyable version is to walk it in pieces across several Peninsula visits, so the familiarity accumulates. Not a race. Dogs not permitted on most sections. Grade: Moderate.

Read the guide →

The Two Bays Walking Trail (26km)

Dromana to Bushrangers Bay. Ancient grasstrees (possibly 200+ years old), eucalypt forest, fern gullies. Two days with camping is better than a single-day push. The grasstrees alone are worth the walk — nothing else on the Peninsula looks like the Two Bays section through Greens Bush in the early morning. Grade: Moderate.

Read the guide →

The Coastal Walk (26–30km)

Cape Schanck to London Bridge. Two days. The Peninsula's wildest walking — Bass Strait clifftops, Bay of Islands, St Paul's, Diamond Bay, Jubilee Point. Exposed and demanding; go prepared. Best in autumn and spring. Summer brings heat and more walkers than the track is designed for. Grade: Moderate–Hard.

Bushrangers Bay Walk (5km return)

The walk that consistently surprises. Cape Schanck carpark to the bay where Main Creek meets the sea — 40 minutes each way, basalt cliffs framing a sandy bay, usually quiet except on summer weekends. Photography walk, easy-to-moderate difficulty, no dogs. Extend to the Two Bays trailhead for an extra 2km. Grade: Easy–Moderate.

Read the guide →

Greens Bush

Victoria's largest surviving coastal heathland remnant. Multiple circuits from Baldry Crossing — the Short (1.6km, 45 min) and Long (3.6km, 1.5 hrs) Baldrys circuits are the entry points. The Long Point Circuit (4km) is the best undervisited walk on the Peninsula. Kangaroos at dusk are a reasonable bet. No dogs. Grade: Easy–Moderate.

Point Nepean Walk (13.5km)

London Bridge to the tip of the Peninsula. Military fortifications from 1878. The quarantine station established 1852. Cheviot Beach where PM Harold Holt disappeared in 1967. The history competes with the view for your attention — which is the highest compliment you can pay a walk. Shuttle bus available. Grade: Easy–Moderate.

Read the guide →

Coppins Track (4km one-way)

Sorrento Ocean Beach to Diamond Bay. The Peninsula's best town-adjacent clifftop walk. Start at Sorrento, walk to Diamond Bay, come back the same way or arrange a pickup. The view from the clifftop above Diamond Bay is one of the Peninsula's most dramatic short-walk rewards. Grade: Easy.

Read the guide →

Arthurs Seat (1.8km summit circuit)

Matthew Flinders Cairn, William Ricketts sculptures, and views that — on a clear day — reach Melbourne, the You Yangs, and Mt Macedon. Moderate difficulty with formed steps. Can combine with the Arthurs Seat Eagle gondola for a one-way walk to the top. Grade: Moderate.

Who's walking

Walks matched to who's coming

Not every walk suits every group. The Peninsula's access rules, terrain, and crowd patterns mean the right walk depends on who is making the trip.

Families

Family Walks

The Cape Schanck boardwalk (20 min, fully accessible) is the best family-appropriate short walk with genuine drama. Mornington foreshore is flat and long enough for varying energy levels. Arthurs Seat TC McKeller Walk is shaded and short. Baldrys Short Circuit in Greens Bush works for older children ready for a proper bush walk. Avoid the clifftop tracks and Coastal Walk section with young children — the exposure and length are not appropriate.

Dog owners

Dog-Friendly Walks

Dogs are banned from Mornington Peninsula National Park (including Cape Schanck, Point Nepean, Two Bays, Coastal Walk) and from Greens Bush and Arthurs Seat State Park. On-lead dogs are generally allowed on bay-side foreshore paths. The Mornington foreshore, Mount Martha clifftop sections, and Rosebud foreshore are your primary options. Verify current conditions with Parks Victoria and local councils before visiting — rules are enforced and change seasonally.

Walkers seeking a challenge

Harder Walks

The Two Bays Walking Trail (26km) is the Peninsula's definitive full-day challenge. The Coastal Walk section (Cape Schanck to London Bridge, 26–30km) is exposed, demanding, and best done over two days. The full Mornington Peninsula Walk (97.5km) is a multi-day circuit requiring serious planning. Single-day hard options include the full Point Nepean walk (13.5km) combined with the Lifesaving Track (11km) as a long day out.

Wildlife watchers

Wildlife Walks

Greens Bush at dawn or dusk for kangaroos, wallabies, and echidnas. The Coastal Walk corridor for Hooded Plovers (nesting season, spring — stay on the track). Mount Martha clifftop between June and September for whale watching vantage points. The Fingal Beach Track for coastal birds in a quieter setting than the main Cape Schanck corridor. Early morning anywhere in the national park for birdlife.

History interest

Historical Walks

Point Nepean for military fortifications, the quarantine station, and the Harold Holt memorial. The Lifesaving Track through the National Park for the 1890s context — the track was built after a series of devastating Bass Strait shipwrecks. Arthurs Seat for William Ricketts sculptures and colonial observation history. Each walk carries a historical layer most visitors do not know to look for.

Photographers

Walks for Photography

Bushrangers Bay for basalt cliff and creek-mouth compositions. Coppins Track for clifftop and Bass Strait light. Diamond Bay from above (access via Coppins or Coastal Walk) for the aerial view down into the bay. Farnsworth Track at low sun for the London Bridge stone arch. Cape Schanck boardwalk at dawn for lighthouse and Bass Strait moody conditions. Greens Bush for grasstree subjects.

Practical notes

Before you go

The Peninsula walks landscape has some consistent conditions that matter regardless of which walk you choose.

Snakes

The Peninsula has Eastern Brown Snakes and Tiger Snakes, both dangerous. Warm months (October to April) require alertness on all bushy and coastal tracks. Stick to the marked track, wear closed shoes, and do not put your hands in grass or under logs. Snake sightings on Greens Bush and the Coastal Walk corridor are not unusual in warm weather.

Sun and Water

The Peninsula's exposed clifftop walks have no shade. In summer, midday is not the right time. Early morning or late afternoon are the better windows — and the light is better for photography. Carry at least 500ml per person for any walk over an hour; none of the major walks have water access en route.

Mobile Coverage

Coverage is variable across the national park. The Coastal Walk corridor and remote sections of Two Bays have dead zones. Download offline maps before you go if you are walking anything more than an out-and-back on a marked track. In an emergency, the parks have emergency contact points at key carparks and trailheads.

Parking

Cape Schanck and London Bridge carparks are busy on summer weekends — arrive before 9am or after 2pm. Parks Victoria charges entry fees at some locations. The Mornington Peninsula National Park entry fee applies across the relevant carparks. Free parking is available at some Greens Bush access points and foreshore locations.

Tide and Conditions

The Coastal Walk section and some beach access points require timing around tides. Check conditions before departing for any walk that involves beach crossings or foreshore sections. Bass Strait weather changes quickly — if conditions look uncertain at the carpark, they are likely worse on an exposed clifftop.

Dogs Banned

Mornington Peninsula National Park (including Cape Schanck, Bushrangers Bay, Point Nepean, Coastal Walk, Two Bays NP sections) — no dogs at all, including in carparks. Greens Bush and Arthurs Seat State Park — no dogs. Bay foreshore areas — generally on-lead permitted. This is the most common planning error for first-time visitors with dogs. Check before you go.

Common questions

What walkers actually ask

What is the best season for walking on the Peninsula?

Autumn (March to May) and spring (September to November) are the best walking seasons. Temperatures are comfortable, crowds are manageable, and the light is excellent for photography. Summer is good for early morning walks on the clifftops before the heat and crowds arrive. Winter is underrated — the Coastal Walk in a moderate southerly swell, in the company of almost no one, is one of the Peninsula's most dramatic walk conditions. Just go prepared for cold and mud on the longer tracks.

Is the 100km walk actually 100km?

No — it is officially 97.5km and called the "Mornington Peninsula Walk." The "100km" name is marketing shorthand that stuck. The circuit combines the Two Bays Walking Trail, the Coastal Walk, the Point Nepean Walk, and the Bay Trail, and takes most walkers 8–10 days to complete. It is best planned in sections across multiple Peninsula visits rather than as a single continuous challenge.

Can I walk from Sorrento to Portsea?

Yes — the Coastal Walk and clifftop tracks connect much of the southern tip. The Farnsworth Track from London Bridge to Portsea Ocean Beach (1.5km one-way) is the clearest direct route in the Portsea direction. Wilson's Folly and other tracks extend the corridor toward Sorrento. Check current track conditions and access via Parks Victoria before combining tracks into a longer point-to-point.

Is the Cape Schanck Lighthouse walk the same as Bushrangers Bay?

No — these are two different experiences departing from close to the same area. The Cape Schanck boardwalk takes 20 minutes return to the lighthouse platform. The Bushrangers Bay walk is 5km return (2 hours) to the bay below the cape, departing from the same carpark. Both are worth doing. Most first-time visitors only do the boardwalk; most returning visitors say the Bushrangers Bay walk is the better experience.
