The Dog-Friendly Peninsula: A Weekend That Actually Works With the Dog

Off-leash beaches, vineyard cellar doors that welcome them, pubs that feed them under the table, and a walk on the Cape that does not end in a hot car. A proper guide to the Peninsula with a dog — and which mistakes to avoid.

By The Peninsula Insider
DogsBeachFamiliesExplorePubsRed HillBalnarringFlinders
A labrador splashing through shallow Peninsula bay water at a dog-friendly beach in the morning light

The Peninsula is one of the most dog-friendly regions in Australia, and it is also the region where dog-weekend planning most often goes wrong.

The reasons are structural. Melbourne has the second-highest dog-ownership rate in Australia, most Peninsula weekends are built around food and wine, and most food-and-wine spots are technically dog-friendly in their outdoor areas only. The trap is simple: you book a table at a cellar door, the weather turns, and you are outside in a rainstorm with a wet retriever while everyone else has moved inside. Or you drive to the back beach at Sorrento with a dog that has been in the car for two hours, and discover that the beach is off-limits between November and April.

None of these problems are unsolvable. They are just problems no one tells you about in advance. Here is the actual guide to doing a Peninsula weekend with a dog that everyone — including the dog — actually enjoys.

The principle: build the day around the dog, not the dog around the day

The single biggest mistake dog-weekend visitors make is fitting the dog into the standard weekend plan. That weekend has a cellar-door lunch, a back-beach walk, a village stroll, and a pub dinner, and it assumes the dog is happy in the car between each stop. Six hours of driving, one hour of walking, cellar door with the dog under the table, a late ferry, and a tired animal by five.

It does not work. Dogs on weekends need more total walking than that plan gives them, and less total sitting-in-a-dining-room time. The version that works inverts the ratio: two proper walks a day, two or three meals that are short and easy, and a base close enough to the walks that the dog is not getting car-sick every time you move.

Where to stay with the dog

The first decision is the base, and it matters more with a dog than without.

Dog-friendly stays with outdoor space and no stairs. The three cottages we recommend for a proper dog weekend are Hillview Cottages near Red Hill, Crittenden Villas on the Dromana side, and the cottages at Sorrento Coastal Retreat — all of which allow dogs with advance notice, have fenced private outdoor areas, and are within ten minutes of at least one off-leash beach. Book early: the pet-friendly rooms sell out faster than the regular ones.

What to avoid. Any hotel with carpeted corridors and a small “pet-friendly” wing miles from the lobby. Dogs do not cope well with stairs and lifts in unfamiliar buildings, and the distance from the room to the nearest bit of grass becomes the whole logistics problem of the weekend. Pick cottages, not hotels.

The Peninsula’s best dog beaches

Most bay-side beaches on the Peninsula are dog-friendly in the off-season and during restricted hours in summer. The ones worth building a weekend around are the ones that allow off-leash access morning and evening year-round.

Balnarring Beach is the default. The northern end of the beach allows off-leash access for dogs between roughly dawn and 10am, and again after about 7pm, year-round. The water is shallow, the sand is fine, and the back beach area behind the ti-tree scrub has a small walking trail that works well as a cool-down loop afterwards. It is also the quietest of the good dog beaches.

Safety Beach — the foreshore between Martha Cove and Mount Martha — has a dedicated off-leash area along a kilometre-long stretch of bay coast, open year-round. The sand is the pale shallow kind that dogs love running through, and the parking is plentiful.

Rye Front Beach (the bay side, not the ocean side) allows off-leash access on sections of the beach outside summer peak hours. This is useful for a late-afternoon walk when the front-beach crowds have thinned.

Cape Schanck and Gunnamatta ocean beach areas permit dogs on-leash only, and not in the National Park sections. Be specific about where you walk — the Point Nepean National Park, the Bushrangers Bay walk, and most of the Cape Schanck walking corridor ban dogs entirely. The Coppins Track in Sorrento, through the heath between the front and back beach coasts, is one of the few Peninsula National Park walks that allows dogs on-leash year-round.

The rule: assume an on-leash restriction on any track that is National Parks managed, and an off-leash opportunity on any Council-managed foreshore at the permitted hours. Check signs.

The vineyards and breweries that actually welcome dogs

This is where the Peninsula becomes properly useful for dog visitors. There is a short list of cellar doors and breweries with genuine, structural dog-welcoming culture — grass areas, water bowls at the bar, staff who expect the dog, and outdoor covered space if the weather turns.

Red Hill Brewery is the best of them. A low barn cellar door on the side of a hop garden, grass tables, a wood-fired pizza oven, and staff who will bring a bowl of water to your table without being asked. Dogs are welcome throughout the outdoor area, which is extensive, and there is enough undercover space that you are not kicked out by the first shower. This is the long-lunch spot for a dog weekend.

Merricks General Wine Store also welcomes dogs in the outdoor paddock area. The wood oven handles the kitchen side and the dining room inside is visible enough through the windows that the dog can settle on grass while you eat on the verandah with a clear sightline. Works best outside peak summer.

St Andrews Beach Brewery is the third option — the beer hall in a converted horse stable at St Andrews, with a huge grassed area around it, and dogs on grass is very much the expected mode. Worth the slightly longer drive from Red Hill if you want a bigger open space for the dog to move around in.

Montalto welcomes dogs in the sculpture grounds (not the restaurant), which means you can walk the sculpture trail with the dog before eating, then leave the dog in the car for a short lunch — a version that works if your travel companion can do a shorter sitting.

The pubs

Pub food with a dog is one of the under-appreciated Peninsula moves. Four pubs do this job cleanly:

Balnarring Pub has an outdoor beer garden that is dog-welcome year-round, with decent pub food and a very local atmosphere. Ten minutes from Balnarring Beach. This is the most obvious mid-weekend dog-and-pub move.

Flinders Hotel has a broad verandah and a beer garden that welcomes dogs. The food is a cut above most pubs on the Peninsula, and the village setting means you can park once, have lunch, and walk the dog around Flinders before driving anywhere.

Dromana Hotel has a small grass side area and welcomes dogs there. Useful for the northern end of the Peninsula and convenient if you are based at Dromana or Mount Martha.

Rye Hotel and Sorrento Hotel are both dog-welcoming in their outdoor courtyards but are busier in summer and harder to get a settled corner in.

A proper dog weekend plan

Here is the version of the weekend we recommend for a couple with a medium-sized dog, arriving from Melbourne on a Friday afternoon.

Friday

  • 3.30pm — Check in to the cottage. Walk the dog around the property.
  • 4.30pm — Drive to Balnarring Beach for a long off-leash run. The beach is at its best with low sun. Let the dog get properly tired.
  • 6pm — Back to the cottage, cold drink, feed the dog.
  • 7pm — Dinner at Balnarring Pub on the outdoor beer garden with the dog at your feet.

Saturday

  • 7.30am — Early coffee and walk. If you are based in Red Hill, drive down to the Balnarring Beach off-leash area for a second walk. If you are based further north, use Safety Beach or Mornington foreshore.
  • 9am — Mornington Farmers Market if it is the second Saturday of the month (dogs welcome on leash at the outdoor market grounds, plenty of space).
  • 11am — A short coffee at Commonfolk in Mornington (outdoor tables dog-friendly) or at Somers General.
  • 12.30pm — Lunch at Red Hill Brewery. Budget two hours. Grass, pizza, beer flight, dog dozing beside the table.
  • 3pm — Main Ridge Dairy for a cheese pick-up on the way back.
  • 3.30pm — Nap time for everyone at the cottage.
  • 5pm — A second walk. Coppins Track in Sorrento is the right length for a late-afternoon stretch. On-leash.
  • 7pm — Easy dinner close to base. A plate of cheese from the dairy, bread from Flinders Sourdough, and a bottle from the cellar door. The dog sleeps.

Sunday

  • 8am — Long walk at your best dog beach. If the weather is on, Balnarring again. If not, the bay foreshore at Mount Martha is the reliable alternative.
  • 10am — Coffee in a cafe with outdoor tables.
  • 11.30am — Flinders Hotel beer garden for an early lunch with the dog.
  • 1pm — Drive home, beating the Sunday traffic, with a dog that has had two long walks every day and no unhappy surprises.

The things to skip

  • Red Hill cellar doors that are indoors-only. Not all of them welcome dogs in their outside areas. Check before you book.
  • Point Nepean, Bushrangers Bay, and Cape Schanck lighthouse walks. Dogs are not permitted in the National Park sections. Pick Coppins Track or the council-managed foreshore tracks instead.
  • Summer peak hours at any front beach. Dog access is restricted in the middle of the day between November and April; go at dawn or dusk.
  • A standard cellar-door-to-cellar-door day. Most serious cellar doors in Red Hill are not dog-welcoming, and trying to turn the weekend into a wine tour with a dog in tow is the version that ends in frustration. Pick the two or three that work and commit.

The argument

The Peninsula with a dog is not a compromised version of the Peninsula weekend. It is, done properly, a better version — more walking, more beach, more grass, less sitting, and a rhythm that keeps both humans and animals moving through the day instead of slumping through it. The trick is choosing the venues that genuinely expect the dog rather than the ones that tolerate it, and building the plan around the walks first and the bookings second.

Come on the right weekend, pick the right base, and the dog will have a better time than the humans do. Which is, in many households, the actual definition of a successful holiday.

Beach
Balnarring 60 min · Easy

Balnarring Beach

Balnarring Beach is Western Port's quiet argument against the bay-side beaches most visitors default to. The sand is paler, the water flatter, and the crowd is almost entirely local — a few families on summer weekends, a scattering of walkers the rest of the year. The beach itself runs for kilometres, with ti-tree scrub backing the dunes and almost no built infrastructure. This is the Peninsula beach people come to when they want to swim without any of the theatre. Good for a slow afternoon between a Merricks lunch and a Balnarring produce stop. Very good for dusk walks in autumn when the light turns mauve across the water. Go through the Balnarring Beach hamlet for the easiest access point. Bring your own chair. Don't expect a kiosk.

Walk
Cape Schanck 90 min · Moderate

Bushrangers Bay Walk

Bushrangers Bay is the walk we send people to when they say they want to see the wilder side of the Peninsula without committing to a full-day hike. The track drops from the Cape Schanck lighthouse precinct through coastal scrub and opens onto a broad crescent of basalt and sand that feels much further from Melbourne than it is. The return climb is enough to justify lunch afterwards, but not so punishing that it tips into chore. Do it in the late afternoon when the light starts to flatten across the water and the whole coastline turns silver.

Lookout
Cape Schanck 35 min · Easy

Cape Schanck Boardwalk

The Cape Schanck boardwalk is short, obvious, and absolutely worth doing. A lot of Peninsula viewpoints require an act of faith before they pay off; this one gives you the basalt formations, the pounding water, and the lighthouse almost immediately. Think of it as the Southern Ocean aperitif before a longer walk or a late lunch inland. It is particularly good for visitors who want scale and coastline without needing to dedicate half the day to it.
