The Peninsula Insider — Strategic Review & Transformation Blueprint

Prepared for: James Richmond, Owner Prepared by: Editorial & Brand Strategy (Remy) Date: 9 April 2026 Classification: Board‑grade strategic deliverable Scope: Full-site teardown, competitive benchmark, future-state blueprint, prioritised roadmap Estimated read: 25–30 minutes

1. Executive Summary

The Peninsula Insider today is a well-designed, editorially-capable starter built on a content foundation that is already better than 80% of regional tourism sites in Australia — and structurally incapable of ever becoming what it wants to be.

It looks like a media brand. It behaves like a directory. That contradiction is the single biggest problem on the site, and until it is resolved, every other decision — more venues, a map, a newsletter, a booking widget — will be a marginal improvement on an unclear foundation.

Let me be direct about what's working, what isn't, and what the real opportunity is.

What's genuinely good

  • Visual identity and design system. The "Curated Journal" direction — Fraunces + Outfit, warm wine-country palette, grain texture overlay, restrained micro-interactions — is editorially confident and distinct from every competitor in the market. This is the foundation you build on, not around.
  • Editorial voice when it appears. The eat page, wine page and walks intros have moments of real authority — "don't try to visit more than three in a day… the peninsula rewards those who slow down" — that sound like a writer who actually lives there. This is the most defensible asset on the site.
  • Depth in two categories. Eat (85 venues, 11 tiers) and Wine (45 wineries, 7 tiers) are now genuinely serious. Nothing else on the Australian tourism map has this combination of curation + density for the Peninsula.
  • Insider framing. "The Peninsula Insider" is a strong brand position. It promises what nobody else credibly delivers: a local's guide rather than a tourist board.

What is critically broken

  • The content model is an unresolved hybrid. It's trying to be a magazine, a directory, a guide and a brochure at the same time. Every page is a card-grid index. No venue pages. No articles. No seasonal journal. No editorial streams. No story you can read start-to-finish.
  • No atomic content. Every restaurant, winery, beach, walk and hotel is a paragraph inside a card on a long index page. You cannot link to it, rank it in Google, share it, save it, or build an itinerary around it. This single architectural choice caps your SEO, your shareability and your product ceiling.
  • Stay page is a skeleton — six listings vs. 85 on Eat. For a destination where accommodation is the highest commercial-value page, this is an unforced error.
  • No maps, no bookings, no saves, no accounts, no personalisation, no real newsletter. All four of the missing product layers that would turn this from a brochure into a media brand + booking platform.
  • Homepage does the least editorial work of any page. It is a set of six teasers and a Jackalope shout-out. No cover story, no seasonal lead, no point of view.
  • "Insider" is a naming promise the site doesn't keep. Right now it is a very well-written brochure. An Insider brand should be the most human, most opinionated, most obviously-written-by-someone voice in the space. It needs a byline, a perspective, and the ability to say "this is overrated."

The transformation opportunity

The Mornington Peninsula has no category-leading media brand. Visit Mornington Peninsula is a legacy directory. Broadsheet treats the Peninsula as a sub-region of Melbourne, not a destination of its own. Urban List is shallow listicles. Condé Nast, Afar and the global premium players do not cover this market with any depth. There is a clear, winnable position: the definitive editorial + utility brand for the Mornington Peninsula, built like a premium travel media company, monetised through affiliate bookings, partnered stays, sponsored journal features, a paid newsletter product and a ticketed events / experiences layer.

None of this is possible on the current foundation. All of it is possible within 6–12 months on the right one.

Overall transformation potential: 10/10.

The brand, the voice and the ground truth (the Peninsula itself) are all in place. The site is the limit, not the idea. Rebuild the architecture, ship atomic content, add maps, add bookings, publish like a media brand, and this is a category-leadership business in twelve months. Keep adding cards to index pages and it will plateau in six.

The rest of this document is the teardown, the benchmark, and the build order.

2. Current Site Critique

Read against the standard of "category leadership in a premium travel category," not against the standard of "better than most regional tourism sites."

2.1 Copy — What it's doing well

  • Voice is present. There is a real writer here. Lines like "let time dissolve," "the peninsula rewards those who slow down," "arrive relaxed and let the conversation happen," "as close as the peninsula gets to eating in a Tuscan barn" — this is editorially confident, sensory, slightly romantic. It is not ChatGPT pablum. Preserve this at all costs.
  • Occasional moments of real insight. Insider tips like "book Doot Doot Doot before you confirm your Jackalope room" or "book Laura at lunch rather than dinner" are the most valuable words on the site — they are exactly the things a concierge tells friends. These are your proof-of-promise.
  • Restraint. Very few exclamation marks. Very little superlative stacking ("AMAZING!! UNMISSABLE!!"). That alone puts it ahead of every tourism board in the country.

2.2 Copy — What it's doing badly

  • Generic at the edges. "World-class dining," "rugged coastline," "charming coastal villages," "crystal clear waters," "genuinely lovely," "unspoiled beaches," "surprisingly special." Half the copy has been sand-blasted into the same beige paragraph that every tourism brochure in the world writes. Where the eat page gets specific ("caviar cameos," "chef-hatted Italian-inflected cooking," "30 seats only"), the homepage drifts into cliché.
  • Overview tiles on the homepage are genuinely weak. "200+ wineries. Hot springs. Dual coastline. World-class dining. Red Hill Market. 90 min from Melbourne." This is the copy of a brochure printed in 2009. A category-leading site should not have the words "world-class" anywhere on it. What does "world-class" mean? Name names. Show me the hat count. Tell me which chef left Attica to open a 30-seater. Specificity is the whole game.
  • No emotional architecture. There is no arc from dreaming → desire → planning → booking. Copy drifts between inspiration ("wild surf, hot springs and long lunches collide") and utility ("90 min from Melbourne via Nepean Hwy") without choreography. A premium travel site is a seduction funnel. This one is a set of landing bays.
  • No narrator. There is no "I," no byline, no "we." The site has a voice but no face. Pickings from the eat page — "as close as eating in a Tuscan barn" — is the kind of line a named editor writes, not an anonymous CMS.
  • No admission of a negative. The site never once says what's overrated, what's touristy, what to skip, where the service drops off in summer, which weekends are impossibly booked, which cellar door was great two years ago but isn't now. The word "Insider" in the brand name promises exactly this honesty. The copy delivers none of it. This is the fastest possible brand credibility cheat — say one hard true thing on every page.
  • The Red Hill refrain. Read the site start to finish and count how many paragraphs mention Red Hill. The Peninsula has 29 towns and the site treats Red Hill and Merricks as if they were the entire map. This is a commercial signal (the paying venues cluster there), but it reads as editorial bias. Sorrento, Portsea, Flinders, Point Leo, Rye, Mornington, Mount Martha, Hastings all get short shrift.
  • Conversion intent is absent. Almost nothing on the site asks you to do anything. The CTAs are mostly "View all [thing]." No "book now," no "save to trip," no "add to itinerary," no "subscribe for the weekly autumn guide," no "unlock the full Insider list."

2.3 Content model — What kind of site is this actually?

This is the single most important question and the site refuses to answer it. Right now the content model reads as:

LensWhat the site looks like
Is it a brochure?No, there's too much specificity on Eat/Wine.
Is it a directory?Functionally yes — every page is a card-grid index.
Is it a guide?It wants to be. The intros try to guide. The structure doesn't.
Is it a portal?No — nothing is gated, no accounts, no saves, no user data.
Is it a media brand?No — there are no articles, no author, no publishing cadence.
OverallAn unclear editorial-ish directory pretending to be a guide.

The answer the site should be able to give — and does not — is: "We are a local's editorial guide that also works as a booking-enabled planning tool." That one sentence, ruthlessly applied, would reshape almost every page.

2.4 Audience alignment

The site implicitly talks to one persona: a Melbourne couple, 35–55, planning a weekend away, staying at Jackalope, eating at Doot Doot Doot, drinking Pinot. That persona is real and valuable. It's also far too narrow for the traffic potential of the Peninsula, and there is no second audience pathway anywhere — no family mode, no dayripper mode, no foodie deep-dive, no locals' directory, no international visitor pathway, no accessibility pathway, no weekend-of-the-month concierge feel.

2.5 Experience quality

  • Navigation is clean and well-considered. Three-group dropdown (Plan / Experience / Explore) works.
  • Typography is excellent. Spacing is good. The Pixel pass shows.
  • Cards are visually consistent but visually identical — a restaurant card, a walk card, a winery card and a hotel card all look the same. The eye has no rhythm break.
  • Mobile is adequate; nav is usable; nothing about mobile feels considered for mobile. No sticky "near me" bar, no bottom-nav, no thumb-reach actions.
  • Images are stock (Unsplash). On a premium travel brand this is the single most visible tell that the product is not yet what it wants to be. Stock images are forgivable on Day 1 and brand-destroying on Day 180.
  • No maps. For a region that is literally a geography, this is the most consequential missing feature.
  • No search depth. The search modal exists but searches card text on a single page's index. There is no entity-level search.
  • No page-level entities. You cannot link to "Jackalope Hotel" as a URL. You can link to stay.html#card-jackalope-feature. That is a disqualifying gap for SEO, social sharing, and any future booking integration.
  • Events page is chronological and flat. No filters by town, no weekend-of view, no map, no "this weekend" landing state, no booking pathway.
  • Itinerary page is a single pre-built couples weekend hard-coded to April and Jackalope. There are no alternatives, no personalisation, no interaction, no way to save or export.
  • Stay page is thin (6 listings) and feels like a Jackalope promo with supporting cast. No filters, no price anchors, no booking paths, no 100-property deep catalogue.

2.6 Missed opportunities

  • Authority. The site never proves it knows more than you. No "Winemaker's pick: Rollo Crittenden on three wines to try tonight." No "Brigitte Hafner's three dishes at Tedesca that you cannot get anywhere else in Australia." No first-person, attributable claims.
  • Trust markers. No press coverage, no awards, no hat counts visible, no Halliday ratings made primary, no "as featured in" strip. When the same few venues earn most of the national press in the Peninsula, make that explicit.
  • Commerce pathways. No affiliate to Stayz, Booking.com, hotel direct, Resy/Tock, Sculptureinthevines tickets, Peninsula Hot Springs booking, ferry tickets, wine tour operators. Every one of these is a natural commission stream the site is currently leaving on the table.
  • Recurrence. The site is built for first-visits. There is no ongoing reason to come back — no updates feed, no "new this month," no dated editorial, no "the weekend of April 18," no subscription. This is a mortal flaw.
  • Sense of place. For a region as photogenic as the Peninsula, there is astonishingly little photography, no video, no maps, no audio, no field-reportage. A premium travel brand earns sense of place through images. This site doesn't.

Strategic implication: The site does not need more venues. It needs more modes. The transformation is less about adding content and more about adding intent-aware pathways through the content that already exists (and is being written faster than it is being activated).

4. Competitive Pattern Analysis

I benchmarked against twelve operators — a mix of direct, adjacent, and aspirational — and extracted what each does well that Peninsula Insider currently doesn't. Naming names.

4.1 Direct / regional competitors

Broadsheet (broadsheet.com.au/mornington-peninsula) — the most instructive competitor because they are already in this market, they have brand equity, and they are also structurally limited.

What Broadsheet does well:

  • Town × category matrix. Every venue lives at /mornington-peninsula/{town}/{category}/{venue}. /mornington-peninsula/red-hill/restaurants/tedesca-osteria. This is a cleaner URL model than anything Peninsula Insider has. It is automatically SEO-valuable, shareable, and trivially extensible.
  • Editorial layered on a directory. Every venue has both a directory listing and articles that cite it. "Now Open: Aurora," "Five to Try," "A Summer Road-Trip Guide" — Broadsheet generates a constant drip of editorial that re-promotes the directory.
  • Named writers, dated articles. Every piece has an author and a date. That is editorial trust built into the template.
  • "Access" membership play — a paid membership is visible from the header. They've already figured out how to monetise loyalty.

What Peninsula Insider can beat them on:

  • Depth and curation. Broadsheet's Mornington Peninsula restaurants guide lists 19 venues. Peninsula Insider has 85. Broadsheet's wineries guide lists 13. You have 45. The raw curation is already yours — you just need to make it addressable.
  • Local voice and ownership. Broadsheet Peninsula is written by Melbourne-based staff. A real Insider brand can outflank them on "actually from here."
  • Editorial sophistication on slow content. Broadsheet is a news engine. It's weak on evergreen "definitive guide to X" content. A local, slow-moving, authoritative Peninsula Insider can outrank them on evergreen search terms forever.

Urban List (theurbanlist.com/melbourne/.../mornington-peninsula-guide) — the dominant listicle play. Shallow, fast, SEO-optimised, frictionless. Not a real threat at the premium end but they own the mid-funnel "top 10 things to do" search layer.

What to steal: fast title formats, aggressive internal linking between listicles, Instagram-first image treatment. What to reject: the sameness, the shallow treatment, the absence of any editorial point of view.

Visit Mornington Peninsula (visitmorningtonpeninsula.org) — the official regional tourism board. Owns "official" authority and gets top-of-funnel traffic. A textbook legacy directory — endless subcategories, generic filler, no editorial voice. A slow, structurally dated product. Not a real threat to a modern competitor with a voice, but it owns certain key SEO phrases by default.

What to steal: nothing except the taxonomy discipline — they have Things-To-Do/Spa-Wellbeing, Things-To-Do/Breweries-Distilleries, Places-To-See/Beaches-Coastline etc. The clean, extensible structured taxonomy is something Peninsula Insider currently does not have.

4.2 Aspirational competitors — international editorial

Condé Nast Traveler (cntraveler.com) — the global benchmark for premium travel editorial.

Patterns to steal:

  • Cover-story homepage. Nast Traveler runs an editorial front page: a dominant feature story, a secondary story, a rotating news module, a "gold list" of hotels, a guides section, and utility modules (deals, newsletters). Peninsula Insider's homepage is a set of category teasers. Make it a magazine cover.
  • "Gold List" / authority rankings. Once a year, editors rank the best hotels, restaurants, experiences. The list becomes a perennial traffic magnet. A Peninsula Insider Gold List — "The 25 Peninsula Institutions, 2026" — would be one of the most linkable, shareable assets you can build in a week.
  • Destination microsites. For major destinations, CNT builds a mini-site with a landing page, guides, longform, "best of" rankings, hotel coverage. Peninsula Insider should treat each major town (Red Hill, Sorrento, Flinders, Portsea, Mornington) as a destination microsite.
  • Editor bios and bylines. Every article attributed. The brand is the collective of its writers.

Afar (afar.com) — premium but more experiential. Closer to the tone Peninsula Insider wants.

Patterns to steal:

  • "Where to stay, where to eat, what to do" landing template as a modular pattern, reused for every destination.
  • Experience-first taxonomy. Afar organises by how you want to travel, not what is there. "For wellness travellers," "for food lovers," "for design obsessives." Peninsula Insider should have experience pathways alongside place pathways.

Atlas Obscura (atlasobscura.com) — the definitive "hidden gems" publisher. Database-backed editorial with 25,000+ place pages.

Patterns to steal:

  • The database as the product. Every entity is a page. Atlas Obscura has a page for each obscure place, with user-submitted tips, related places, "been there," and tags. It's a directory that reads like a magazine.
  • Tags and taxonomies as first-class navigation. "Abandoned," "botanical," "underground," "oddities." You can rebrand these for the Peninsula: "bayside," "hinterland," "ocean-facing," "family-proof," "walk-in friendly," "sunset spot," "dog-friendly."
  • "Been here / want to visit" interactions. Simple, low-friction account gestures that create returning-user value.

The Infatuation (theinfatuation.com) — the gold standard for restaurant authority.

Patterns to steal:

  • "Where to go for…" landing pages. "Where to go for first dates in Brooklyn." "Where to go for impressive dinner in Melbourne." This is an intent-based index that dominates Google. Peninsula Insider should have: "Where to go on the Peninsula for… anniversary dinner / long lunch with friends / rainy day with kids / a first date / pre-ferry lunch / catching up with parents / vegan dinner / oysters and Champagne / best pinot / a big birthday."
  • Numerical cuisine ratings and confident opinion. Infatuation scores restaurants. They say "the food is 7/10 but the vibe is a 10/10." This kind of specificity is the opposite of "world-class dining."
  • No reservation system of their own — but every review links out to OpenTable/Resy. Peninsula Insider should mirror this ruthlessly.

Eater regional guides (eater.com/maps) — the restaurant mapping standard.

Patterns to steal:

  • Map-first storytelling. Every Eater guide is a map with pinned, numbered entries. The map IS the article. For a region defined by geography, this is the most obvious format Peninsula Insider is missing.
  • "Heatmap" format — the places to eat right now vs. the permanent list. A living, maintained feed.

Tablet Hotels (tablethotels.com) — premium hotel curation meets booking.

Patterns to steal:

  • Curation as product. Tablet handpicks ~1,000 hotels globally. That's the model Peninsula Insider should adopt: not every hotel on the Peninsula, but the hotels on the Peninsula, editorially defended.
  • First-party bookings with hotel partnerships. Every booking is a commission. Peninsula Insider should negotiate direct partnerships with the top 20 properties for editorial coverage + booking commission.

4.3 Adjacent inspiration — tourism boards done well

Visit California (visitcalifornia.com) — the benchmark for a destination marketing site that feels like a publication. Fast, visual, video-first, editorially rich, excellent regional microsites, strong seasonal editorial. Their road trip builder is the cleanest on the internet.

Tourism New Zealand / 100% Pure NZ (newzealand.com) — best-in-class for conveying brand personality at destination-board scale. Heavy narrative, heavy photography, strong regional pathways. Their "Must-dos" framing is editorially simpler than Peninsula Insider's "Things to Do," and more authoritative.

Visit Scotland (visitscotland.com) — best-in-class itinerary builder. Users pick interests and the site assembles a multi-day route. Also strong on seasonal content and editorial "stories from Scotland."

Visit Norway (visitnorway.com) — best-in-class for modular landing pages and experience-led taxonomy. Their "Cabin life," "Winter magic," "Summer hiking" pages are templates you can steal directly.

4.4 Newsletter-led models

The Discoverer (thediscoverer.com) — newsletter-first travel brand. Short, daily, visual, voice-led. Proves a newsletter can be a destination in itself. Peninsula Insider's footer "Subscribe" form is the weakest newsletter CTA on the whole site. The newsletter should be a first-class product, not an afterthought.

Substack travel writers. Multiple travel writers now operate on Substack with paid tiers. "Peninsula Insider" is a perfect Substack-adjacent brand — a named editor, weekly dispatch, member-only content, paid tier for full access to the restaurant book and annual guides.

4.5 What every high performer understands that this site doesn't

  1. Every entity is a URL. Not a card inside a page. A page.
  2. Editorial and directory are the same thing. Venues live in a structured database and show up in dozens of editorial contexts. Not two parallel systems.
  3. The map is core product, not a nice-to-have. In any geography-led category, the map is a competitive moat.
  4. Recurrence beats acquisition. Category leaders make readers come back. They publish. They have a cadence. Peninsula Insider has none.
  5. The homepage is a cover, not a sitemap. Premium publishers use their homepage to say something. This homepage shows a nav bar.
  6. Authority is explicit. Named writers, dated content, award badges, hat counts, press citations, ratings. This site hides almost all of its authority.
  7. Commerce is editorial. Every recommendation has a booking path. Every link is monetised. Without being tacky.
  8. Sense of place is earned with visuals. Original photography and video is not a nice-to-have — it is the brand on a premium travel site.
  9. Experiences beat locations. Travellers search "where to go for a long lunch" more than "restaurants in Red Hill." Site structure should mirror how people think, not how a tourism board organises information.
  10. Accounts enable everything. Saves, trip planning, favourites, "been there," lists, notifications. Without accounts, you are a read-only brochure.

5. Future-State Content Strategy

Stop thinking of Peninsula Insider as a website. Start thinking of it as a local media company with four content surfaces and three commercial engines.

5.1 Positioning statement (recommended)

The Peninsula Insider is the definitive, local, opinionated guide to the Mornington Peninsula — a place to dream about your next weekend, plan it properly, and book the best of it in one move.

Three words do the load-bearing work: local (not generic), opinionated (not neutral), definitive (not surface). Everything on the site must earn one of those adjectives or get cut.

5.2 Signature content pillars (five)

Pillar 1 — Eat & Drink (the flagship) Already your strongest section. Extend it into:

  • Full restaurant database (currently 85, target 200 — every restaurant that matters).
  • Winemaker profiles (40+ long-form interviews).
  • Cellar door book (already at 45 wineries — complete to all 60+).
  • Producer stories: olive oil, cheese, lamb, seafood, truffles, berry farms.
  • "Where to go for…" intent-based landing pages (20+).
  • Seasonal picks: "What to drink on the Peninsula this autumn."
  • An annual authority ranking: "The Peninsula 50" — the fifty most important food & drink venues of the year. Publish in November for the summer season.

Pillar 2 — Stay (the commercial engine) Currently the weakest section and the highest-revenue opportunity.

  • Full hotel/B&B/villa/cottage/glamping database (target 80+ editorial listings).
  • Tier model: The Icons / The Design Hotels / The Hideaways / The Family Houses / The Farm Stays / The Villas / The Glamping / The Surprise Value.
  • Partnered stays program: 20 top properties with editorial features, booking integration, commission deals, concierge add-ons.
  • "The Peninsula Stay Index" — an annual ranking.
  • Per-property long-form features with original photography.

Pillar 3 — Experience (the intent layer) Replace "Explore" with a cleaner experience taxonomy.

  • Wellness (hot springs, spas, baths — with Alba, Peninsula Hot Springs, Aurora at Sorrento, plus private day spas).
  • Nature (walks, beaches, national parks, wildlife, sunrise/sunset spots).
  • Wine country (tastings, tours, producer visits).
  • Art & culture (sculpture parks, galleries, writers' week, music, theatre).
  • Water (ferries, dolphin swims, surf, sailing, kayak, snorkelling).
  • Family (kid-tested experiences across every category).
  • Slow pleasures (long lunches, markets, drives, farm stalls).

Pillar 4 — The Insider's Journal (the editorial engine) A dated, authored, published stream of new content. This is the thing that does not exist today and must exist for the brand to be a media property.

  • Weekly cadence: "This Weekend on the Peninsula" — published every Thursday.
  • Monthly cadence: "The Month Ahead" — published first of each month.
  • Seasonal cadence: "The Autumn Guide / The Winter Guide / The Summer Guide."
  • Longform drops: A proper 3,000-word feature every 3–4 weeks. "I spent a year eating at every restaurant in Red Hill — here are the ones that matter." "A weekend at Jackalope in shoulder season." "The ten winemakers who are changing the peninsula right now."
  • Interview series: "Winemakers & Chefs." Named, photographed, in-depth.
  • First-person columns: A named editor (you or a hired writer) with a byline and a point of view.

Pillar 5 — The Planner (the utility layer) Take the current itinerary page — which is one static weekend — and turn it into a proper planning tool.

  • Multiple pre-built itineraries (12+ at launch, 24+ at maturity) across modes: Couples / Families / Food & Wine / Wellness / Adventure / Locals / Day Trip / Long Weekend / Rainy Day / With Friends / Luxury / Budget / Seasonal variants.
  • Interactive "build your own" planner (pick mood + length + stay base → receive a suggested itinerary).
  • "This weekend" dynamic page based on what's on, weather, market schedule.
  • Save-to-trip functionality (requires accounts).
  • Export / share / send to partner.

5.3 What to deprioritise

  • Homepage hero overview tile. "200+ wineries / Hot Springs / Dual Coastline." Cut it. These things will be demonstrated by the content, not announced by a tile.
  • Flat events lists. The What's On page in its current form does not scale. It should become a database, a map, a filter and an editorial stream.
  • Standalone "Things to Do" heading. It's a lazy taxonomy. Replace with experience pillars.
  • Generic walks / beaches listings. Keep the walks deep-dive (it's actually strong) but reframe as "On Foot" within the Nature experience pillar, and let the map do the work of discovery.

5.4 Editorial principles (non-negotiable)

  1. Every piece is bylined and dated.
  2. Every piece must have a point of view. Not "a charming seaside village" — "Sorrento in February is a cursed nightmare of traffic and overbooked restaurants; Sorrento in April is the version you wish you'd seen."
  3. Every piece must name names. Specific venues, specific people, specific dishes, specific wines.
  4. Admit something true on every piece. Say what's overrated. Call out service drops. Explain why something is worth the drive. This is the core of the Insider brand.
  5. Every recommendation is bookable. If it has a cellar door, link to the booking engine. If it has rooms, link to the booking engine. If it has Resy/Tock, link out.

6. Future-State Information Architecture

  • Keep: the warm wine-country palette. Subtle, not loud. Premium.

7.3 Card systems — differentiate by type

Right now every card looks identical. This is template efficiency but editorial weakness. Introduce type-specific card variants:

  • Venue card (restaurant/winery/stay): image primary, name, place, signature tag, one-sentence pitch, price/booking affordance, "save" icon.
  • Article card: image, byline, eyebrow (category), headline, dek, date.
  • Experience card: image, type icon, name, duration, difficulty/price, save icon.
  • Itinerary card: mood badge, duration, title, "For couples looking for slow indulgence."
  • Event card: dated chip, title, venue, short description, book-ticket CTA.

Small visual variations reinforce the site's intelligence: "this is a place," "this is a story," "this is a plan."

7.4 Typography approach for editorial

  • Articles should use Fraunces at ~22–24px body with generous leading (1.8) and max 680px prose width. Real magazine measure. Currently the prose-width is there (--prose-w: 720px); use it.
  • Pull quotes are already supported in the design system. Use them aggressively in longform.
  • Drop caps on first paragraph of longform articles.
  • Photo captions in a smaller Outfit size with a credit line. Every image.

7.5 Image and video treatment

  • Mandatory: commission original photography for the top 30 venues (every hat, every signature winery, every "icons" hotel). Budget: ~$15–20k one-time investment. This single decision is the most brand-defining spend on the whole transformation.
  • Launch a video pillar — 60-90 second hero videos of signature experiences (sunset at Cape Schanck, the Jackalope drive-in, a morning at Peninsula Hot Springs). Use them as home-page covers and meta-image previews.
  • Every venue page: 5–8 images minimum, hero image, gallery scroll, credited.

7.6 Maps — the single biggest product upgrade

The site currently has zero maps. This is the biggest gap and the most transformative addition.

  • Full-screen map experience at /map — Mapbox or MapLibre, custom-styled in the brand palette.
  • Every entity pinned. Filters by type, place, mood.
  • Map on every venue page — embedded 300px tall, "Directions" CTA.
  • Map on every itinerary page — route-based, numbered stops.
  • Map on the events page — "what's on near me this weekend."
  • Place-based pages (/eat-drink/red-hill) show a map pinned to that place's venues.

A custom-styled map is 10x the brand cue that generic pins are. Commit to a week of design work here.

7.7 Mobile-first considerations

  • Sticky bottom bar on mobile: Home / Eat-Drink / Stay / Plan / Map. Thumb-reach.
  • One-tap call/directions/book on venue pages.
  • "This Weekend" modal — single-tap access to tonight/tomorrow/Sunday picks.
  • Swipe gestures on cover story, itinerary carousel.
  • Save-for-later bookmarks tied to local storage (or account).

7.8 Conversion touchpoints

Every page has a clear primary action:

  • Venue pages → book (outbound affiliate).
  • Article pages → subscribe to newsletter.
  • Itinerary pages → "Save trip" (account creation).
  • Event pages → buy tickets / add to calendar.
  • Homepage cover → read the story.

Currently no page has a primary action. That's the single biggest conversion issue.

7.9 Avoiding generic / template feel

  • Editorial moments — break the grid. A feature article should break out of the 1100px container for a full-bleed image. An itinerary should feel different from a directory page.
  • Branded micro-interactions — subtle: the grain overlay, a hover letterspace, a fade-up reveal. Never gimmicky.
  • Confident whitespace. The current site already respects this. Do more.
  • Named signatures — "As chosen by [editor]," "As photographed by [photographer]." People trust people.

  • Framer Motion or custom micro-interactions kept very restrained.

  • Atomic pages — every venue/experience/article is its own URL from day one. Infinite scale.
  • Tag-driven landing pages — /eat-drink/for/long-lunch is auto-generated from the database by filter. Add a mood tag and you've created a new page with zero engineering.
  • Programmatic content opportunities — every {place}/{type} combination can be programmatically generated. Red Hill + restaurants. Sorrento + cafes. Flinders + walks. That's 29 places × 8 types = 232 potential landing pages, auto-generated, for SEO.
  • Internal link graph auto-built from tag overlaps. No manual work.

8.5 SEO architecture

  • Clean URLs as above.
  • Rich schema — Restaurant, LodgingBusiness, TouristAttraction, LocalBusiness, Event, Article schema on every entity. Enables Google rich results.
  • Canonical tags to prevent duplicate indexing.
  • Sitemap auto-generated from the CMS.
  • OpenGraph + Twitter cards with real per-page images.
  • Breadcrumbs (already present in current design — keep them).
  • Semantic HTML throughout (most of the current site is adequate).

8.6 Personalisation opportunities (phase 2)

  • Accounts: save venues, build itineraries, mark "been there," follow authors, receive personalised newsletters.
  • Seasonal personalisation: home cover story adapts to visitor history.
  • Location-aware: "You're 15 minutes from Ten Minutes by Tractor — they have a walk-in window at 1pm."

8.7 Map / data integration

  • Google Places API for richer data on venue pages (hours, reviews, price). Cached, not live.
  • OpenWeather / BOM for "This weekend" weather module.
  • Searoad ferry API if available, or scraped schedule for "next ferry" widget.
  • Tide data for beach-goers.
  • Sunrise / sunset — trivial calculation, surprisingly valuable for the "sunset spot" mood tag.

8.8 Event infrastructure

The What's On page today is a flat HTML list. Future state:

  • Events as first-class entities with start/end dates, venue reference, ticket links.
  • Calendar view + list view + map view.
  • Filter by category, place, price, date range.
  • "This weekend" dynamic page pulled from the same database.
  • Related venues auto-populated (where to eat near this event, where to stay).

8.9 Editorial workflow

  • Editorial calendar visible to all contributors.
  • Assignment → draft → edit → publish with named roles.

  1. Cut the homepage overview tile ("200+ wineries / Hot Springs / …") and replace with a single editorial sentence and a photograph.
  2. Add bylines and dates to every existing page. Even if the byline is just "By The Peninsula Insider — Updated April 2026."
  3. Write one "Where to go for…" page. Start with "Where to go for a long lunch on the Mornington Peninsula."
  4. Replace the Unsplash hero image on the homepage with one commissioned Peninsula photograph.
  5. Rewrite the footer subscribe form with a named newsletter product and stronger CTA.
  6. Audit every sentence of the overview copy ("world-class," "charming," "rugged") and replace with specific claims.
  7. Add a "Last updated: 9 April 2026" note to every page.

Appendix B: Things to Stop Doing

  • Stop adding cards to index pages. Every new card is debt.
  • Stop using stock photography anywhere the brand is visible.
  • Stop being neutral. Every recommendation without a "but…" is a missed opportunity.
  • Stop treating Red Hill and Jackalope as the default. Expand the geography and the stay options.
  • Stop writing "things to do" as a heading. It's a lazy taxonomy.
  • Stop hiding authority signals. If a restaurant has a hat, show the hat. If a winemaker won James Halliday's top 100, show it.
  • Stop treating the newsletter as a footer afterthought. It is the single most strategic asset you can build.
