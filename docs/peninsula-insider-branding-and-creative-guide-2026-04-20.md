# Peninsula Insider — Branding & Creative Guide

**Prepared for:** James Richmond  
**Prepared by:** Remy  
**Date:** 20 April 2026  
**Status:** Canonical working guide for editorial, design, social, newsletter, and campaign execution

---

## 1. Purpose

This document is the working source of truth for how Peninsula Insider should look, sound, and feel.

It exists to give the editorial team, design team, social team, newsletter team, and any supporting agents one shared standard.

Peninsula Insider should feel like:
- a premium editorial publication
- a trusted local guide
- a sharp, selective cultural lens on the Mornington Peninsula

It should not feel like:
- a tourism board
- a brochure site
- a generic lifestyle publisher
- a discount-led travel marketplace
- a content farm dressed up as a magazine

This guide is intended to make the brand legible and repeatable across:
- the website
- newsletter and email assets
- social media
- editorial packages
- branded documents
- campaign creative
- future product surfaces

---

## 2. Brand core

### 2.1 Brand idea

**Peninsula Insider is the insider guide to the Mornington Peninsula.**

Not the biggest directory.
Not the official voice.
Not the most generic.

It wins by being:
- more selective
- more tasteful
- more useful
- more local
- more opinionated
- better at helping people decide what is actually worth their time

### 2.2 Mission

To be the definitive, trusted, editorially led guide to the Mornington Peninsula, helping readers shape better days, weekends, stays, meals, outings, and rituals.

### 2.3 Positioning

Peninsula Insider sits at the intersection of:
- **editorial judgement**
- **local intelligence**
- **practical trip-shaping utility**

The brand promise is not “everything on the Peninsula.”

The promise is:

> **The places, experiences, and decisions actually worth your time.**

### 2.4 Brand role in market

Peninsula Insider is not competing to be the official source.
It is competing to be the preferred source.

That means:
- more trust than a tourism board
- more specificity than a lifestyle publisher
- more taste than a directory
- more utility than a magazine alone

---

## 3. Audience

Primary audiences:
- Melbourne couples planning a Peninsula day or weekend
- high-intent food, wine, and stay seekers
- repeat Peninsula visitors who want better judgement, not more noise
- locals looking for what is actually worth doing this week

Secondary audiences:
- families planning weekends or school-holiday outings
- dog owners
- event and wedding researchers
- corporate retreat planners
- golf, spa, and walking audiences

The audience is not looking for exhaustive coverage.
They are looking for a better filter.

---

## 4. Brand personality

Peninsula Insider should feel:
- informed
- calm
- selective
- elegant
- local
- literate
- observant
- useful
- premium without flash

Peninsula Insider should not feel:
- loud
- salesy
- over-excited
- generic
- influencer-coded
- listicle-churned
- luxury-for-luxury’s-sake
- brochure-safe

### 4.1 Personality shorthand

If the brand were a person, it would be:
- the friend with excellent taste
- the local who knows what to skip
- the editor who can shape a weekend in three sentences
- the guide who is warm, but not gushy

---

## 5. Brand pillars

### 5.1 Local intelligence
The brand must feel grounded in the Peninsula itself, not written from a distance.

### 5.2 Editorial judgement
The brand does not dump options. It curates, prioritises, compares, and recommends.

### 5.3 Practical usefulness
The brand must help readers act, not just admire.

### 5.4 Taste without pretension
The brand can be premium, but it cannot become snobbish, cold, or exclusionary.

### 5.5 Calm confidence
The brand should sound certain and composed, not hyped or defensive.

---

## 6. Voice and tone

### 6.1 Core voice

The voice should be:
- clear
- elegant
- concise
- specific
- sensorial when it matters
- lightly opinionated
- practically helpful

The voice should avoid:
- filler hype
- fake warmth
- generic tourism phrases
- exaggerated luxury language
- SEO sludge phrasing
- purple prose with no utility payoff

### 6.2 Tone by use case

#### Homepage / front door
Tone: composed, invitational, editorial, confident

#### Journal articles
Tone: more voiced, more interpretive, more shaped, but still useful

#### Service pieces / guides
Tone: clear, selective, structured, quietly decisive

#### Newsletter
Tone: intimate, editorial, companionable, lightly insiderish

#### Social media
Tone: sharper, more immediate, but still recognisably Peninsula Insider

#### Corporate / partner-facing docs
Tone: strategic, precise, premium, not inflated

### 6.3 Voice rules

Do:
- make real recommendations
- use specific nouns
- say what a place is good for
- help readers compare options
- admit tradeoffs
- sound like the publication has standards

Do not:
- write like an ad
- use empty superlatives
- pretend everything is equally good
- say “world-class dining” or similar brochure filler
- pad copy with vague aspiration

### 6.4 Signature voice pattern

A strong Peninsula Insider sentence often does one of these:
- names the occasion
- identifies the right fit
- frames the tradeoff
- makes a tasteful recommendation
- tells the reader how to shape the day well

Example pattern:
- not just what it is
- but who it suits, when it works, and why it matters

### 7.1 Core brand line

**The insider guide to the Mornington Peninsula.**

This remains the cleanest, strongest master line.

### 7.2 Supporting message territories

#### Territory 1: Trusted local judgement
We help people choose well.

#### Territory 2: Better Peninsula days
We shape not just destinations, but days, weekends, and moods.

#### Territory 3: Editorial curation over directory overload
We do not try to show everything. We show what matters.

#### Territory 4: Peninsula intelligence with style
We combine practical value with aesthetic confidence.

### 7.3 Short brand descriptors
Use variations like:
- The insider guide to the Mornington Peninsula
- Trusted Peninsula picks on food, wine, stays, walks, events, and places
- A local, editorial guide to what is actually worth your time on the Peninsula

## 8. Visual identity principles

### 8.1 Visual ambition

The visual system should feel like:
- a premium editorial travel publication
- a modern magazine with regional depth
- wine-country restraint rather than coastal cliché
- thoughtful, text-led design supported by strong imagery

It should not feel like:
- a tourist brochure
- a generic WordPress template
- a real estate portal
- a resort ad campaign
- a maximalist lifestyle brand

### 8.2 Visual traits

The design language should be:
- warm
- spacious
- textured
- typographic
- calm
- quietly cinematic
- understated but deliberate

### 8.3 Core visual promise

The visual system should communicate:
- this brand has taste
- this brand is readable
- this brand knows the Peninsula
- this brand can hold both inspiration and utility without visual chaos

---

## 9. Colour world

The colour system should stay inside a Peninsula editorial palette:
- warm cream / parchment
- stone / sand neutrals
- sea-fog blue-grey
- olive and muted eucalyptus greens
- pinot / oxblood / deep wine reds
- charcoal and ink neutrals

### 9.1 Colour behaviour

Use colour to create:
- warmth
- contrast
- hierarchy
- editorial mood

Do not use colour to create:
- loud urgency
- retail-sale energy
- playful tourism-board brightness

### 9.2 Accent usage

Wine-toned accents should be selective and premium.
Greens should feel botanical and muted, not fresh-and-flashy.
Blue tones should feel coastal and weather-softened, not tropical.

---

## 10. Typography

Current typography direction is correct and should remain the baseline.

### 10.1 Typographic roles

- **Serif display:** authority, editorial presence, cover-story energy
- **Sans serif:** readability, utility, interface clarity, supporting structure

### 10.2 Typography principles

The typography should do a lot of the brand work.
It should signal:
- modern editorial confidence
- readability first
- premium restraint
- hierarchy without noise

### 10.3 Typographic rules

Do:
- use elegant serif moments for masthead, feature headlines, and section-defining copy
- keep body copy highly readable
- let whitespace and scale create emphasis

Do not:
- over-style small text
- stack too many font weights in one module
- create fussy magazine pastiche
- make utility surfaces harder to scan in the name of style

---

## 11. Layout and composition

### 11.1 Layout principles

Layouts should feel:
- editorial
- intentional
- spacious
- legible
- not over-packed

### 11.2 Composition rules

Use:
- generous spacing
- clear hierarchy
- clean content bands
- restrained section transitions
- calm surfaces with one primary focal point

Avoid:
- too many competing cards in one viewport
- cluttered promotional stacking
- overly dashboard-like front-door sections
- thin text over visually noisy imagery

### 11.3 Front-door rule

The homepage must read like an editorial front door first, and a content index second.

---

## 12. Photography and image direction

### 12.1 Photography role

Photography should do three jobs:
- anchor the sense of place
- support editorial quality
- strengthen trust and specificity

### 12.2 Desired image feel

Images should feel:
- real
- place-grounded
- textural
- atmospheric
- observational
- seasonally aware
- editorial rather than promotional

### 12.3 Favour imagery that shows

- coastline with recognisable Peninsula character
- vineyards, dining rooms, village textures, walks, hinterland roads
- soft weather, changing light, off-peak nuance
- details that feel locally observed

### 12.4 Avoid imagery that feels

- generic stock travel
- tropical or irrelevant coastal fantasy
- over-saturated tourism-board gloss
- too posed
- too ad-like
- semantically mismatched to the content

### 12.5 Image integrity rule

Images must always match the actual subject, category, and promise of the page.
Low-match imagery damages trust.

### 12.6 Generative image rule

Generative imagery is only acceptable as controlled editorial atmosphere for non-documentary slots.
It is not acceptable as fake evidence of a real place.

## 13. Editorial design system

### 13.1 What Peninsula Insider pages should balance

Every key surface should balance:
- inspiration
- selection
- usability
- readability
- trust

### 13.2 Section experience standard

Every major hub should feel like:
- a polished editorial category
- not a flat listing dump

This means:
- a clear point of view
- shaped curation
- selective modules
- meaningful cross-linking
- premium metadata and preview treatment

### 13.3 Article standard

Articles should feel like:
- real editorial assets
- not SEO pages with prettier headings

A strong article page should include:
- high-quality hero treatment
- elegant introduction
- clear section rhythm
- practical outcomes for the reader
- related next-step guidance

## 14. Newsletter and email creative system

### 14.1 Newsletter role

The newsletter is not a bolt-on CTA.
It is a branded editorial product.

### 14.2 Email feel

Peninsula Insider email assets should feel:
- editorial
- premium
- composed
- visually aligned with the site
- useful enough to open regularly

### 14.3 Email creative rules

Use:
- the same colour world as the site
- the same typographic logic
- the same calm editorial spacing
- selective, high-quality imagery
- clean modular sections

Avoid:
- marketplace email clutter
- hard-sell growth-hack patterns
- over-designed promo blocks
- generic SaaS email styling

### 14.4 Newsletter product personality

Emails should read like:
- the publication arriving in your inbox
- not a funnel step disguised as content

---

## 15. Social creative system

### 15.1 Social role

Social should make Peninsula Insider feel:
- current
- saveable
- tasteful
- useful
- habit-forming

### 15.2 Social visual standard

Social should carry the same brand logic as the site:
- calm authority
- place-first imagery
- restrained typography
- editorial rather than creator-style packaging

### 15.3 Social content mix

The best mix is:
- editorial framing
- standout recommendations
- saveable utility
- weekend decision support
- lived Peninsula atmosphere
- reflective recap

### 15.4 Social design rules

Do:
- adapt platform formats without breaking the brand
- keep text-on-image minimal unless utility requires it
- treat carousels as editorial objects, not noisy promo slides

Do not:
- flood creative with stickers, badges, arrows, or emoji-led clutter
- make every post a link dump
- let LinkedIn become watered-down Instagram copy

## 17. Brand application by surface

### 17.1 Website
Must feel like the main expression of the brand.
Everything else should echo it.

### 17.2 Email
Should feel like the website in inbox form.

### 17.3 Social
Should feel like Peninsula Insider translated for each platform, not redesigned for each platform.

### 17.4 Documents and internal decks
Should reflect the same strategic seriousness and taste level as public brand materials.

### 17.5 Future product surfaces
Search, app, concierge, workbench, and membership surfaces should all inherit the same core brand behaviours:
- calm
- selective
- premium
- useful

## 18. Creative approval checklist

Before shipping any public-facing asset, check:

### Brand fit
- Does this feel like Peninsula Insider?
- Does it feel editorial, local, and selective?
- Does it avoid brochure energy?

### Visual fit
- Does the palette align with the existing brand world?
- Is typography calm and premium?
- Is the layout clean and readable?

### Editorial fit
- Is the tone confident and specific?
- Is the recommendation or framing actually useful?
- Is there any generic filler language that should be cut?

### Image fit
- Does the image genuinely match the subject?
- Does it strengthen trust?
- Is it strong enough for a premium editorial brand?

### Channel fit
- Has the asset been adapted properly for the platform?
- Does it still feel coherent with the master brand?

---

## 19. Non-negotiables

These are hard rules.

- No brochure language.
- No semantically weak imagery.
- No generic tourism-board creative.
- No over-designed promo clutter.
- No fake-local tone.
- No treating every venue, town, or event as equally worthy.
- No public-facing asset that feels off-brand just because it is quick.

---

## 20. Working creative test

A Peninsula Insider asset is on-brand if it makes a reader feel:

- these people know the Peninsula
- these people have taste
- these people can help me choose well
- this feels better than generic travel content

If it does not produce that feeling, it needs another pass.
