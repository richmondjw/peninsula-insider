The Mornington Peninsula wine region is often introduced as a single place. It isn't. It's nine distinct sub-regions spread across 792 hectares of vines, from Moorooduc near Frankston in the north to Main Ridge and Balnarring in the south. Where you drink on the Peninsula depends on what you're after: serious benchmark Pinot Noir in the cool hills of Main Ridge, design-forward architecture and hatted restaurants in Merricks North, or dog-friendly lawns and easy walk-in cellar doors closer to the bay.

No vineyard on the Peninsula is more than 7km from the ocean. That maritime influence — cool summers, mild winters, consistent sea breezes — is why cool-climate varieties work so well here. Pinot Noir accounts for roughly 50% of plantings and is the regional benchmark. Chardonnay follows. Pinot Gris has a disproportionate presence: Kathleen Quealy of Quealy Winemakers is credited as the Australian pioneer of the variety, planting on the Peninsula since 1993.

## The sub-regions

Understanding the Peninsula's geography is the most useful thing you can do before planning a visit. The cellar doors are spread across a broad area — not a single wine trail — and each sub-region has its own character.

### Red Hill South
The plateau's highest elevations and oldest vines. Where the benchmark Pinot Noir comes from. Highest concentration of hatted restaurants on the Peninsula.

Wineries: **Montalto**, **Port Phillip Estate**, **Paringa Estate**

### Main Ridge
The Peninsula's coolest and quietest sub-region. Minimal signage, rolling hills, serious wine. Ten Minutes by Tractor (first certified organic vineyard on the Peninsula), T'Gallant (Pinot Grigio pioneer, reopened after a 2022 closure), Red Hill Estate, and Main Ridge Estate — the oldest licensed winery on the Peninsula (1975).

Wineries: **Ten Minutes by Tractor**, **T'Gallant**, **Red Hill Estate**, **Main Ridge Estate**

### Merricks
Bay-facing gentle slopes. Pt. Leo Estate (330-acre sculpture park, two-hat Laura restaurant) and Stonier Wines anchor this sub-region.

Wineries: **Pt. Leo Estate**, **Stonier Wines**

### Merricks North
Jackalope Hotel and its two restaurants (Doot Doot Doot and Rare Hare, both one hat GFG 2025) define this sub-region. The design-forward, scene end of the Peninsula.

Wineries: **Jackalope / Rare Hare (Willow Creek vineyard)**

### Red Hill (village)
The Red Hill village zone. Polperro, Foxeys Hangout, Many Little. Foxeys was the first Peninsula winery with full organic and biodynamic certification.

Wineries: **Polperro**, **Foxeys Hangout**, **Many Little**

### Balnarring
North-east area. Quealy Winemakers — the Australian Pinot Gris pioneer. Tasting room only; no restaurant.

Wineries: **Quealy Winemakers**

### Dromana / Tuerong / Moorooduc
The northern belt, more accessible from Frankston and Melbourne. Crittenden Estate (first to commercialise Italian varieties — Nebbiolo, Sangiovese — in Australia), Yabby Lake (single-vineyard Pinot and Chardonnay), Dromana Estate, Moorooduc Estate.

## What to drink

**Pinot Noir.** The Peninsula's signature. Fine tannins, red-fruit character, age-ability. Main Ridge Estate's Half Acre Pinot is a recognised regional benchmark; Ten Minutes by Tractor's Wallis and McCutcheon single-vineyard wines attract serious critical attention; Paringa Estate has accumulated consistent recognition since winemaker Lindsay McCall founded the estate in 1985.

**Chardonnay.** Stonier and Port Phillip Estate are reliable entry points. Yabby Lake under winemaker Tom Carson produces single-block Chardonnay that commands particular critical attention.

**Pinot Gris and Pinot Grigio.** Quealy Winemakers since 1993; T'Gallant (which popularised the Italian-style Pinot Grigio in Australia from the Main Ridge); Pt. Leo Estate.

**Italian varieties.** Crittenden Estate was the first to commercialise Nebbiolo and Sangiovese in Australia. A minor corner of the region but historically significant.

## Cellar doors worth the drive

### Montalto (Red Hill South)
Daily. Hatted restaurant (booking required, lunch Fri–Mon) plus the Piazza walk-in tier (daily 11am–5pm, kitchen to 4pm). The 1km sculpture trail through the property is one of the better free walks on the Peninsula. Best for: long lunch, families, groups.

### Pt. Leo Estate (Merricks)
Daily. Laura restaurant inside (two hats GFG 2025, book four months ahead). Wine Terrace for casual tastings and lunch. 16-acre sculpture park with $15 adult entry. Western Port Bay views from multiple points on the property.

### Ten Minutes by Tractor (Main Ridge)
Daily — but bookings are essential even for the cellar door. Hatted restaurant Wednesday to Sunday lunch; dinner Friday to Saturday. Allis wine bar on Friday–Saturday 11am–9pm and Sunday 11am–6pm for a more informal visit.

### Stonier Wines (Merricks)
Seven days, 11am–5pm. One of the Peninsula's pioneer wineries (founded 1978). Walk-in friendly, dog-friendly lawn, children's playground on-site. No restaurant, but local charcuterie available. Groups of 8+ should contact in advance.

### Main Ridge Estate (Main Ridge)
Friday to Sunday and public holidays, 12–5pm. The Peninsula's oldest winery (licensed 1975). Larder restaurant with share plates, outdoor only. Must book; tick the dog option at booking if bringing a dog (on-lead, outdoor, max 1 dog per booking). Closed Good Friday, Christmas, Boxing Day, ANZAC Day, Melbourne Cup Day.

### Quealy Winemakers (Balnarring)
Daily 10am–5pm. Tasting room only — Kathleen Quealy has been growing Pinot Gris on this site since 1993. No dogs. No groups over 6 unannounced.

### T'Gallant (Main Ridge)
Daily 10:30am–5pm. Reopened mid-2023 after a closure, with a new Bergman & Co design fit-out. Wood-fired pizzeria, piazza seating, weekend live music. Accessible, family-friendly, no booking required.

### Polperro (Red Hill)
Cellar door Wednesday to Sunday 11am–5pm; walk-in, max 6 guests. Hatted restaurant on-site (booking required). Winter fire pits set up across the hillside. Four vineyard villas available for accommodation.

### Foxeys Hangout (Red Hill)
Friday to Monday plus public holidays, 11am–5pm. First Peninsula winery with full organic and biodynamic certification. No bookings accepted; max group size 6; kitchen closes 4pm. No dogs.

## Dog-friendly: confirmed and denied

The following reflects only what can be verified directly from venue websites.

**Confirmed dog-friendly:**
- **Stonier Wines** — lawn is dog-friendly (stated on venue site)
- **Main Ridge Estate** — on-lead, outdoor only; must pre-book with the dog option selected; max 1 dog per booking
- **Green Olive at Red Hill** — outdoor dining areas (multiple sources confirmed)
- **Many Little Bar & Dining** — outdoor seating; enquire when booking

**Explicitly no dogs:**
- **Foxeys Hangout** — "Please leave your lovely dogs at home" (website)
- **Quealy Winemakers** — "We kindly ask no dogs" (cellar door page)

For all other venues: dog policy is unconfirmed. Assume no unless the venue's own website says otherwise.

## Annual events

**Winter Wine Weekend** (King's Birthday long weekend, June): 40+ wineries presenting at Red Hill Showgrounds. The centrepiece of the regional calendar, organised by the Mornington Peninsula Wine Association. Worth planning a visit around.

**Piers & Pinots**: Mornington Peninsula Wine Association event focused on Pinot Noir, Pinot Gris, and Sparkling. Replaces/supplements the former "Pinot Week" branding — check the MPWA website for current dates.

---

## Practical notes

Most cellar doors operate Thursday to Monday or Friday to Monday — Tuesday and Wednesday closures are standard across the region. Check before driving out, especially in winter. Bookings are required or strongly recommended at Ten Minutes by Tractor, Polperro, and Pt. Leo Estate. Stonier and T'Gallant are the most accessible walk-in options with restaurant food on-site.

The distance from Melbourne (~1 hour, Moorooduc Highway) makes this a viable day trip, but two days covers the sub-regions more properly.

## Frequently asked questions

**What is the Mornington Peninsula known for in wine?**
Pinot Noir (~50% of plantings) and Chardonnay. Cool maritime climate, no vineyard more than 7km from ocean. Quealy Winemakers pioneered Pinot Gris in Australia from here; Crittenden Estate was first to commercialise Italian varieties.

**How many wineries?**
200+ vineyard operations; 50+ cellar doors open to the public; 792 hectares of vines.

**Can I bring my dog?**
Stonier, Main Ridge Estate (pre-book), Green Olive, and Many Little are confirmed dog-friendly. Foxeys and Quealy explicitly say no. Verify directly for all others.

**Best time to visit?**
Autumn for harvest atmosphere; June for the Winter Wine Weekend; spring for mild temperatures. Summer is peak season and most crowded.
