# Editor's Letter — 24 April 2026

There is a particular quality of light on Westernport in April that I keep failing to describe accurately. Not gold, exactly. More the colour of patience. The bay has its own rhythms — tidal, seasonal, ecological — that proceed entirely without reference to whatever the rest of the country is doing.

This week, Lucien spent a morning at low tide with Tom Kearney, whose family has been harvesting wild oysters from those flats for three generations. The piece that came back is not a restaurant story. It is a story about water, time, and what it costs to know one place that well. It also happens to be about the finest thing you can eat on the Peninsula right now, and why you probably won't be able to in five years.

That tension — between what is here and what is passing — runs through everything we've commissioned this week. Iris finds the same quality in the studio trail that opens this weekend across forty-plus properties: the works are mostly quiet, mostly made by people who live here, and most of them will not be reviewed by anyone other than you, the reader who walks through the door. Our community piece, by a writer new to these pages, stays with a grief support group that met in a garden and became something its founder never intended.

There is no thesis in this. The Peninsula does not need one. It asks, instead, for the kind of attention that follows things to their source — to the flat at low tide, to the shed where the clay is still wet, to the garden where people come because something has ended and they need somewhere to put their hands.

That is, I think, what we are for.

Tyler Lockhart
*Editor-in-Chief, Peninsula Insider*
